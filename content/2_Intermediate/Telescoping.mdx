## Overview

Many competition problems ask you to evaluate a sum or product whose closed
form is not immediately obvious. A powerful strategy is **telescoping**:
rewriting each term so that most of it cancels with a neighboring term,
leaving only a handful of boundary terms.

The name comes from a collapsible spyglass — you start with something long
and it collapses down to something short. The workflow is always:

1. **Decompose** the general term into a difference (for sums) or ratio (for products).
2. **Write out** several terms to confirm the cancellation pattern.
3. **Collapse** down to boundary terms.
4. **Evaluate** what remains.

## Telescoping Sums

### The Fundamental Idea

If you compute $\sum_{k=1}^{n}(a_k - a_{k+1})$, writing it out gives

$$
(a_1 - a_2) + (a_2 - a_3) + \cdots + (a_n - a_{n+1}) = a_1 - a_{n+1}.
$$

Every intermediate term cancels. The challenge is finding the decomposition
that turns your expression into this form.

### Partial Fractions

The most common tool is partial fraction decomposition. For $\frac{1}{k(k+1)}$,
we solve $\frac{1}{k(k+1)} = \frac{A}{k}+\frac{B}{k+1}$, giving $A=1$, $B=-1$:

$$
\frac{1}{k(k+1)} = \frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+1}.
$$

So $\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k(k+1)} = 1 - \frac{1}{n+1} = \frac{n}{n+1}$.

### Wider Gaps

When factors are not consecutive, cancellation still occurs but with a gap.
For $\frac{1}{k(k+2)} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+2}\right)$,
writing out terms:

$$
\frac{1}{2}\left[\left(\frac{1}{1}-\frac{1}{3}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{4}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{3}-\frac{1}{5}\right)+\cdots\right].
$$

Each positive $\frac{1}{m}$ cancels with the negative $\frac{1}{m}$ appearing
two terms later. The first 2 and last 2 terms survive:

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k(k+2)} = \frac{1}{2}\left(1+\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{n+1}-\frac{1}{n+2}\right).
$$

**General principle:** a decomposition with gap $d$ leaves $d$ boundary terms
at each end.

### Quadratic Denominators

Since $k^2-1=(k-1)(k+1)$, we get gap 2:

$$
\frac{1}{k^2-1} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{k-1}-\frac{1}{k+1}\right).
$$

So $\sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{1}{k^2-1} = \frac{1}{2}\left(1+\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{n}-\frac{1}{n+1}\right)$,
which converges to $\frac{3}{4}$ as $n\to\infty$.

### Rationalizing Radicals

For $\frac{1}{\sqrt{k}+\sqrt{k+1}}$, multiply by the conjugate:

$$
\frac{\sqrt{k+1}-\sqrt{k}}{(k+1)-k} = \sqrt{k+1}-\sqrt{k}.
$$

Then $\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{\sqrt{k}+\sqrt{k+1}} = \sqrt{n+1}-1$.

### Double Telescoping (Cubic Denominators)

For $\frac{1}{k(k+1)(k+2)}$, partial fractions give

$$
\frac{1}{k(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\left(\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+1}\right)-\left(\frac{1}{k+1}-\frac{1}{k+2}\right)\right].
$$

Defining $f(k)=\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+1}$, each term is $\frac{1}{2}(f(k)-f(k+1))$,
which telescopes to

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{(n+1)(n+2)}\right) = \frac{1}{4}-\frac{1}{2(n+1)(n+2)}.
$$

## Telescoping Products

The multiplicative analogue: $\prod_{k=1}^{n}\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k} = \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_1}$,
since each numerator cancels with the adjacent denominator.

**Example.** $\prod_{k=2}^{n}\frac{k-1}{k} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\cdots\frac{n-1}{n} = \frac{1}{n}$.

**Example.** For $\prod_{k=2}^{n}\frac{k^2-1}{k^2}$, factor as
$\frac{(k-1)(k+1)}{k\cdot k}$ and split into two products:

$$
\prod_{k=2}^{n}\frac{k-1}{k}\cdot\prod_{k=2}^{n}\frac{k+1}{k} = \frac{1}{n}\cdot\frac{n+1}{2} = \frac{n+1}{2n}.
$$

## Strategy Checklist

- **Factor the denominator** and look for consecutive integer factors.
- **Apply partial fractions** to express each term as a difference.
- **Write out 4–5 terms** explicitly — do not skip this.
- **Identify the gap** $d$ and track $d$ boundary terms at each end.
- **For products**, rewrite each factor as $\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}$.
- **For infinite sums**, verify the tail terms vanish.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Forgetting boundary terms.** With gap $d$, you must keep $d$ terms at each
  end. Losing one is the most common error.
- **Expanding before factoring.** Multiplying out $(k-1)(k+1)=k^2-1$ just to
  re-factor it wastes effort and invites mistakes.
- **Mismatched indices.** Verify that $\frac{1}{k+1}$ from term $k$ actually
  cancels with $\frac{1}{k+1}$ from the correct neighboring term.
- **Ignoring convergence.** For infinite series, confirm $a_n\to 0$.

## Practice Problems

> Problem: 2025 AMC 10B Problem 15

> The sum $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k^3+6k^2+8k}$ can be expressed as $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime positive integers. What is $a+b$?

> $\textbf{(A)}\ 89\qquad\textbf{(B)}\ 97\qquad\textbf{(C)}\ 102\qquad\textbf{(D)}\ 107\qquad\textbf{(E)}\ 129$

> ### Solution
>
> **Factor the denominator.** $k^3+6k^2+8k = k(k+2)(k+4)$.
>
> **Partial fractions.** Setting $\frac{1}{k(k+2)(k+4)}=\frac{A}{k}+\frac{B}{k+2}+\frac{C}{k+4}$
> and solving (plug in $k=0,-2,-4$) gives $A=\frac{1}{8}$, $B=-\frac{1}{4}$, $C=\frac{1}{8}$:
>
> $$
> \frac{1}{k(k+2)(k+4)} = \frac{1}{8}\left(\frac{1}{k}-\frac{2}{k+2}+\frac{1}{k+4}\right).
> $$
>
> **Regroup for telescoping.** Write $\frac{1}{k}-\frac{2}{k+2}+\frac{1}{k+4}$ as
>
> $$
> \left(\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+2}\right)-\left(\frac{1}{k+2}-\frac{1}{k+4}\right) = f(k)-f(k+2),
> $$
>
> where $f(k)=\frac{1}{k}-\frac{1}{k+2}$. This is a gap-2 telescope in $f$.
>
> **Collapse.** $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}(f(k)-f(k+2)) = f(1)+f(2)$ since $f(k)\to 0$.
>
> $$
> f(1)+f(2) = \left(1-\frac{1}{3}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{4}\right) = \frac{2}{3}+\frac{1}{4} = \frac{11}{12}.
> $$
>
> **Final answer.** $\frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{11}{12} = \frac{11}{96}$, and
> $\gcd(11,96)=1$, so $a+b = 11+96 = \boxed{107}$. The answer is $\textbf{(D)}$.
