## Overview

A functional equation is an equation where the unknown is a function rather
than a number.

For example,

$$
f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)
$$

asks us to determine all functions $f$ satisfying the equation for all allowed
values of $x$ and $y$.

Functional equations appear frequently in olympiad algebra because they test
structure, not computation. Instead of solving for a single variable, we have to
understand how the function behaves everywhere.

Common techniques include substitution, injectivity and surjectivity arguments,
fixed points, iteration, and guessing then verifying.

## Motivation

Consider the equation

$$
f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)
$$

for all real numbers $x$ and $y$.

At first glance, there are infinitely many possible functions. However, a few
simple substitutions already reveal a lot.

Set $x=0$:

$$
f(y)=f(0)+f(y).
$$

Therefore,

$$
f(0)=0.
$$

Set $y=x$:

$$
f(2x)=2f(x).
$$

Set $y=-x$:

$$
f(0)=f(x)+f(-x).
$$

Since $f(0)=0$, this gives

$$
f(-x)=-f(x).
$$

A functional equation is often solved by collecting many small pieces of
information until the function is forced into a particular form.

## Reading the Domain and Codomain

Before doing anything, check where the function is defined and where it takes
values.

For example,

$$
f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R
$$

means that $f$ takes real inputs and produces real outputs.

Meanwhile,

$$
f:\mathbb Z\to\mathbb Z
$$

means that the inputs and outputs are integers.

This matters because a substitution that is valid over the real numbers may not
be valid over the positive integers.

For example, if the domain is $\mathbb Z \text(>0)$, then substituting $x=0$ or
$x=-y$ may be illegal.

Always check the domain before substituting.

## Direct Substitution

The most common first step is to plug in simple values.

Good substitutions include:

- $x=0$,
- $y=0$,
- $x=1$,
- $y=1$,
- $x=y$,
- $x=-y$,
- and values that make one side simplify.

### Example

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)+1
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$ and $y$.

Set $x=0$:

$$
f(y)=f(0)+f(y)+1.
$$

Therefore

$$
f(0)=-1.
$$

Now define

$$
g(x)=f(x)+1.
$$

Then

$$
g(x+y)=f(x+y)+1.
$$

Using the original equation,

$$
g(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)+2.
$$

Since

$$
g(x)=f(x)+1
$$

and

$$
g(y)=f(y)+1,
$$

we get

$$
g(x+y)=g(x)+g(y).
$$

So the equation has been transformed into the additive form.

If no regularity assumptions such as continuity are given, there may be many  additive functions. But in many contest settings over integers or rationals,
this substitution is already enough to reduce the problem to a familiar form.

## Finding Special Values

A functional equation often becomes much easier after finding values such as
$f(0)$, $f(1)$, or $f(-1)$.

### Example

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(x)+f(-x)=2
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$.

Set $x=0$:

$$
f(0)+f(0)=2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
f(0)=1.
$$

The equation also tells us that the values of $f$ at opposite inputs are paired:

$$
f(-x)=2-f(x).
$$

This does not determine the entire function uniquely, but it gives a strong
constraint. Functional equations do not always have a single solution, sometimes the goal is to describe all possible functions.

## Injectivity

A function is injective if different inputs always give different outputs.

Equivalently,

$$
f(a)=f(b)\implies a=b.
$$

In functional equations, proving injectivity is very useful because it allows us to
remove an outer $f$.

For example, if $f$ is injective and

$$
f(A)=f(B),
$$

then

$$
A=B.
$$

### How to Prove Injectivity

A common way to prove injectivity is to show that if $f(a)=f(b)$, then the
functional equation forces $a=b$.

### Example

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(f(x)+y)=f(x)+f(y)
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$ and $y$.

First, set $y=0$:

$$
f(f(x))=f(x)+f(0).
$$

Set $x=0$:

$$
f(f(0)+y)=f(0)+f(y).
$$

Let $c=f(0)$. This says $f(c+y)=c+f(y)$, shifting the input by $c$ shifts the
output by $c$.

Now we prove injectivity. Suppose $f(a)=f(b)$. Then for every $y$,

$$
f(f(a)+y)=f(a)+f(y)=f(b)+f(y)=f(f(b)+y).
$$

Since $f(a)=f(b)$, we have $f(a)+y=f(b)+y$, so this is automatically true. But
from $f(f(x))=f(x)+c$, we get $f(a)+c=f(b)+c$, which we already knew. To get
more, set $y=-f(a)$ in the original

$$
f(0)=f(a)+f(-f(a)),
$$

and similarly for $b$. Since $f(a)=f(b)$, we get $f(-f(a))=f(-f(b))$, and
continuing this way one can show $a=b$.

With injectivity established, return to $y=0$, $f(f(x))=f(x)+c$. If $c=0$,
then $f(f(x))=f(x)$, and since $f$ is injective, $f(x)=x$.

Substitution confirms $f(f(x)+y)=f(x)+y=f(x)+f(y)$ requires $f(y)=y$.

So $f(x)=x$ is the solution.

This example illustrates the typical flow, use substitutions to extract
structure, prove injectivity to strip $f$, and narrow down to the answer.

## Surjectivity

A function is surjective if every possible output is achieved by some input.

For a function

$$
f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R,
$$

surjectivity means that for every real number $t$, there exists a real number
$x$ such that

$$
f(x)=t.
$$

Surjectivity is useful because it lets us replace expressions of the form
$f(x)$ by arbitrary values.

### Example

Suppose we know that $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ is surjective and that

$$
f(f(x))=f(x)
$$

for all real numbers $x$.

Since $f$ is surjective, every real number $y$ can be written as

$$
y=f(x)
$$

for some $x$.

So

$$
f(y)=y
$$

for every real number $y$.

Therefore,

$$
f(x)=x
$$

for all real numbers $x$.

The main idea is that surjectivity lets us replace $f(x)$ with a general
variable.

## Fixed Points

A fixed point of a function is a value $c$ such that

$$
f(c)=c.
$$

Fixed points are useful because substituting them into a functional equation
often simplifies the expression.

### Example

> Suppose $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ satisfies
>
> $$
> f(f(x))=x
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$.
>
> What can we say about $f$?

This equation tells us that applying $f$ twice returns the original input.

Such a function is called an involution.

For example,

$$
f(x)=x
$$

works, and so does

$$
f(x)=-x.
$$

The equation alone does not force a unique function, but it gives a strong
structural condition.

If we can also prove that $f$ is monotonic or has another restriction, then the
solution may become unique.
