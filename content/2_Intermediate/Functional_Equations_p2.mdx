## Iteration

Some functional equations involve repeated application of the function, such as

$$
f(f(x)),
$$

$$
f(f(f(x))),
$$

or even longer chains.

This is called iteration.

### Example

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(f(x))=x+2
> $$
>
> and
>
> $$
> f(x+2)=f(x)+2
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$.

The first equation says that applying $f$ twice increases the input by $2$.

If we guess that $f$ is linear, say

$$
f(x)=ax+b,
$$

then

$$
f(f(x))=a(ax+b)+b=a^2x+ab+b.
$$

We need

$$
a^2x+ab+b=x+2.
$$

As such

$$
a^2=1.
$$

If $a=1$, then

$$
f(x)=x+b.
$$

Then

$$
f(f(x))=x+2b,
$$

so $b=1$.

This gives

$$
f(x)=x+1.
$$

If $a=-1$, then

$$
f(x)=-x+b.
$$

Then

$$
f(f(x))=x,
$$

which does not work.

So the natural linear solution is

$$
f(x)=x+1.
$$

Substitution confirms that it satisfies both equations.

## Symmetry

Many functional equations contain two variables and remain similar after
swapping them.

For example, if an equation holds for all $x$ and $y$, then it also holds after
exchanging $x$ and $y$.

### Example

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)
> $$
>
> and
>
> $$
> f(xy)=f(x)f(y)
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$ and $y$.

Set $y=0$ in the first equation:

$$
f(x)=f(x)+f(0),
$$

so

$$
f(0)=0.
$$

Set $y=1$ in the second equation:

$$
f(x)=f(x)f(1).
$$

If there exists some $x$ with $f(x)\neq 0$, then

$$
f(1)=1.
$$

Now set $x=y=1$ in the first equation:

$$
f(2)=f(1)+f(1)=2.
$$

Also by repeated addition,

$$
f(n)=n
$$

for all positive integers $n$.

This example shows how combining equations and simple substitutions can build
information in increments.

A full classification over the real numbers requires more care, but contest
problems often include enough restrictions to force the intended solution.

## Guess and Verify

In many intermediate problems, the solution is a simple function such as:

$$
f(x)=x,
$$

$$
f(x)=x+c,
$$

$$
f(x)=cx,
$$

or

$$
f(x)=ax+b.
$$

It is often useful to guess a possible form, substitute it into the equation,
and then prove that no other functions work.

### Example

> Find all linear functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)+3
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$ and $y$.

Assume

$$
f(x)=ax+b.
$$

Then

$$
f(x+y)=a(x+y)+b=ax+ay+b.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
f(x)+f(y)+3=(ax+b)+(ay+b)+3=ax+ay+2b+3.
$$

Thus

$$
b=2b+3.
$$

So

$$
b=-3.
$$

There is no restriction on $a$, so all linear solutions are

$$
f(x)=ax-3.
$$

Substitution confirms that all such functions work.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(x+y)=f(x)+y
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$ and $y$.

Set $x=0$:

$$
f(y)=f(0)+y.
$$

Let

$$
c=f(0).
$$

Then

$$
f(y)=y+c.
$$

So the only possible functions are

$$
f(x)=x+c.
$$

Substituting back:

$$
f(x+y)=x+y+c
$$

and

$$
f(x)+y=x+c+y.
$$

They are equal, so every function

$$
f(x)=x+c
$$

works.

### Example 2

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)
> $$
>
> and $f(1)=3$, assuming $f$ is linear.

Since $f$ is linear, write

$$
f(x)=ax+b.
$$

Set $x=0$ in the functional equation:

$$
f(y)=f(0)+f(y),
$$

so

$$
f(0)=0.
$$

So

$$
b=0.
$$

Since

$$
f(1)=3,
$$

we get

$$
a=3.
$$

Therefore,

$$
f(x)=3x.
$$

Substitution confirms that this function works.

### Example 3

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ satisfying
>
> $$
> f(f(x))=x
> $$
>
> and
>
> $$
> f(x)+x=0
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$.

The second equation gives

$$
f(x)=-x.
$$

Now check the first equation:

$$
f(f(x))=f(-x)=x.
$$

So

$$
f(x)=-x
$$

works.

### Example 4

> Find all functions $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that
>
> $$
> f(xf(y)+x)=xy+f(x)
> $$
>
> for all real numbers $x$ and $y$.

Set $x=1$:

$$
f(f(y)+1)=y+f(1).
$$

This tells us that $f$ takes every value of the form $y + f(1)$, which is
every real number. So $f$ is surjective.

It also tells us that $f$ is injective, if $f(a)=f(b)$, then $f(a)+1=f(b)+1$,
so $f(f(a)+1)=f(f(b)+1)$, giving $a+f(1)=b+f(1)$, hence $a=b$.

Now set $y=0$:

$$
f(xf(0)+x)=f(x).
$$

Since $f$ is injective, we can remove $f$ from both sides:

$$
xf(0)+x=x.
$$

For all $x \neq 0$, this gives $f(0)=0$.

Set $x=1$ again, $f(f(y)+1)=y+f(1)$. Setting $y=0$: $f(f(0)+1)=f(1)$, so
$f(1)=f(1)$, which is consistent.

Now guess $f(x)=x$. Then $f(xf(y)+x)=xy+x$ and $xy+f(x)=xy+x$. These match,
so $f(x)=x$ is a solution.

To confirm uniqueness, from $x=1$ we have $f(f(y)+1)=y+f(1)$. Setting
$y=-f(1)$ gives $f(f(-f(1))+1)=0$, so $f(-f(1))+1=0$ (since $f$ is injective
and $f(0)=0$), meaning $f(-f(1))=-1$. Continuing with specific substitutions,
one can show $f(1)=1$, then $f(n)=n$ for all integers, extend to rationals, and
use monotonicity (which follows from the original equation) to conclude
$f(x)=x$ for all real $x$.

This example combines multiple techniques, one substitution gives surjectivity
and injectivity, another uses injectivity to remove $f$ and find $f(0)$, and
guess and verify is used to complete it.

## Key Contest Insight

Functional equations are usually solved by building information gradually.

A typical solution does not usually come from only one substitution. Instead, you
collect facts such as:

- the value of $f(0)$,
- whether $f$ is injective,
- whether $f$ is surjective,
- how $f$ behaves at special inputs,
- and whether the equation suggests a linear or constant form.

Once a candidate function is found, always substitute it back into the original
equation to verify that it works.

## Strategy Tips

- Start with simple substitutions such as $0$, $1$, $x=y$, and $y=-x$.
- Check whether the domain allows your substitution.
- Try to find $f(0)$ and $f(1)$ early.
- If you see $f(A)=f(B)$, consider proving injectivity.
- If every value can be written as $f(x)$, use surjectivity.
- If the equation contains $f(f(x))$, think about iteration.
- If a simple function seems likely, guess it, test it, and then prove it is
  forced.
- Always verify all proposed solutions.

## Common Pitfalls

- Substituting values that are not in the domain.
- Assuming the function is continuous or linear when this is not stated.
- Proving that a function is possible but forgetting to prove uniqueness.
- Forgetting to substitute the final answer back into the original equation.
- Cancelling or applying injectivity before it has been proved.
- Ignoring constant solutions.
