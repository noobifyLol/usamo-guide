## Overview

Angle bisectors turn angles into side ratios. You will need them for area and segment-ratio problems, and frequently appear in AMC 8, AMC 10, and basic olympiad geometry.

The most important result is the Angle Bisector Theorem, which changes angle information into side-length information. This makes many geometry problems simple algebra.

## Definitions and Key Ideas

Consider triangle $ABC$, and let the bisector of $\angle A$ intersect side $BC$ at $D$.

The Angle Bisector Theorem states that

$$
\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{AB}{AC}.
$$

In words, an angle bisector divides the opposite side in the ratio of the adjacent sides.

Because triangles $ABD$ and $ACD$ share the same altitude from $A$, their areas satisfy

$$
\frac{[ABD]}{[ACD]} = \frac{BD}{DC}.
$$

Combining this with the Angle Bisector Theorem gives

$$
\frac{[ABD]}{[ACD]} = \frac{AB}{AC}.
$$

This is one of the fastest shortcuts for area-ratio problems.

Another useful fact is that every interior angle bisector meets the opposite side between its endpoints, dividing the triangle into two smaller triangles.

## The Angle Bisector Theorem

If $AD$ bisects $\angle A$ in triangle $ABC$, then

$$
\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{AB}{AC}.
$$

This theorem is often used together with

$$
BD+DC=BC
$$

to find unknown segment lengths.

For example, if

$$
AB=8,\qquad AC=6,
$$

then

$$
BD:DC=8:6=4:3.
$$

If $BC=14$, then

$$
BD=8,\qquad DC=6.
$$

## Area Ratios

One of the most common AMC applications of angle bisectors involves areas.

Since triangles $ABD$ and $ACD$ share the same altitude from $A$,

$$
\frac{[ABD]}{[ACD]} = \frac{BD}{DC}.
$$

Using the Angle Bisector Theorem,

$$
\frac{[ABD]}{[ACD]} = \frac{AB}{AC}.
$$

Thus, if

$$
AB:AC=3:5,
$$

then

$$
[ABD]:[ACD]=3:5.
$$

No area formulas are required.

## Recognizing Angle Bisector Problems

Common clues that suggest using the Angle Bisector Theorem include:

- A segment is explicitly described as an angle bisector.
- Two side lengths adjacent to an angle are given.
- A ratio on the opposite side is requested.
- Area ratios appear alongside an angle bisector.
- A triangle is divided into two smaller triangles by a segment from a vertex.

Whenever you see an angle bisector and side lengths together, try the theorem immediately.

## Common Pitfalls

- Using the theorem on a median instead of an angle bisector.
- Mixing up side lengths and segment lengths.
- Writing

$$
\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{BC}{AC}
$$

instead of

$$
\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{AB}{AC}.
$$

- Forgetting to use

$$
BD+DC=BC
$$

when the entire side length is known.

- Applying the theorem without first confirming that the segment is actually an angle bisector.

## Worked Example

In triangle $ABC$, $AB=8$, $AC=6$, and $BC=7$. The bisector from $A$ meets $BC$ at $D$. Find $BD$.

$$
\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{8}{6} = \frac{4}{3}.
$$

Since

$$
BD+DC=7,
$$

the segments are $4$ and $3$, so

$$
BD=4.
$$

## More Examples

### Example 1: Quick Ratio

In triangle $ABC$, $AB=5$ and $AC=10$. The angle bisector from $A$ meets $BC$ at $D$. Find $BD:DC$.

By the Angle Bisector Theorem,

$$
BD:DC = AB:AC = 1:2.
$$

### Example 2: Area Ratio

If

$$ 
[ABD]=12\qquad\text{and}\qquad[ACD]=18,
$$

find $BD:DC$.

The triangles share an altitude from $A$, so

$$ 
BD:DC = 12:18 = 2:3.
$$

### Example 3: Finding Segments

In triangle $ABC$, $AB=12$, $AC=18$, and $BC=20$. The angle bisector from $A$ meets $BC$ at $D$. Find $DC$.

The Angle Bisector Theorem gives

$$
BD:DC = 12:18 = 2:3.
$$

Since

$$
BD+DC=20,
$$

the side is divided into $5$ equal parts.

Each part has length $4$, so

$$
DC=12.
$$

### Example 4: Area From Side Ratios

In triangle $DIH$, the angle bisector from $D$ meets $IH$ at $C$. Suppose

$$
DI=9,\qquad DH=6,
$$

and

$$
[DIH]=30.
$$

Find $[DIC]$.

Since

$$
DI:DH=9:6=3:2,
$$

we have

$$
[DIC]:[DHC]=3:2.
$$

The total area is $30$, so

$$
[DIC] = \frac{3}{5}\cdot30 = 18.
$$

## Strategy Checklist

- Confirm the segment is an angle bisector.
- Immediately write

$$
\frac{BD}{DC} = \frac{AB}{AC}.
$$

- Use

$$
BD+DC=BC
$$

when a full side length is given.

- Convert area information into side ratios whenever possible.

- Look for shared altitudes when comparing triangle areas.
