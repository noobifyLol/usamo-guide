## Useful Binomial Identities

The Binomial Theorem gives several important identities.

### Sum of Binomial Coefficients

Set $a=1$ and $b=1$ in the Binomial Theorem:

$$
(1+1)^n
=
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}
=
2^n.
$$

This says that the sum of the entries in row $n$ of Pascal's Triangle is $2^n$.

### Alternating Sum

Set $a=1$ and $b=-1$

$$
(1-1)^n
=
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}(-1)^k.
$$

For $n>0$, this gives

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n}(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}
=
0.
$$

This means the sum of the even indexed binomial coefficients equals the sum of
the odd indexed binomial coefficients.

### Weighted Sum

Another useful identity is

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n}k\binom{n}{k}=n2^{n-1}.
$$

One way to see this is combinatorially, count the number of ways to choose a
committee from $n$ people and then choose a leader from that committee.

If the committee has size $k$, there are

$$
\binom{n}{k}k
$$

ways.

On the other hand, choose the leader first in $n$ ways, and then choose any
subset of the remaining $n-1$ people to join the committee. This gives

$$
n2^{n-1}
$$

ways.

So

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n}k\binom{n}{k}=n2^{n-1}.
$$

## Estimating and Approximating Powers

The Binomial Theorem can also be used to approximate powers.

For small values of $x$,

$$
(1+x)^n
=
1+nx+\binom{n}{2}x^2+\binom{n}{3}x^3+\cdots.
$$

If $x$ is very small, the higher powers of $x$ may be much smaller than the
first few terms.

For example,

$$
(1.01)^5
=
1+5(0.01)+10(0.01)^2+10(0.01)^3+5(0.01)^4+(0.01)^5.
$$

The first few terms already give a strong approximation

$$
(1.01)^5\approx 1+0.05+0.001=1.051.
$$

In olympiad problems, this idea is more commonly used for exact expansion than
decimal approximation, but the intuition is still useful.

## Modular Applications

The Binomial Theorem is often useful in modular arithmetic.

### Example 5

> Find the remainder when $101^5$ is divided by $100$.

Write

$$
101=100+1.
$$

Then

$$
101^5=(100+1)^5.
$$

Using the Binomial Theorem,

$$
(100+1)^5
=
1+5(100)+10(100)^2+10(100)^3+5(100)^4+(100)^5.
$$

Every term except the first is divisible by $100$, so

$$
101^5\equiv 1 \pmod{100}.
$$

Thus the remainder is $1$.

### Example 6

> Show that $10^{n}-1$ is divisible by $9$ for every positive integer $n$.

Write

$$
10=9+1.
$$

Then

$$
10^n-1=(9+1)^n-1.
$$

By the Binomial Theorem,

$$
(9+1)^n
=
1+\binom{n}{1}9+\binom{n}{2}9^2+\cdots+9^n.
$$

Subtracting $1$ gives

$$
10^n-1
=
\binom{n}{1}9+\binom{n}{2}9^2+\cdots+9^n.
$$

Every term on the right is divisible by $9$, so $10^n-1$ is divisible by $9$.

## Combinatorial Interpretation

The Binomial Theorem is not just an algebraic formula. It also has a counting
interpretation.

When expanding

$$
(a+b)^n,
$$

we are multiplying $n$ copies of

$$
(a+b).
$$

Each factor gives us two choices: choose $a$ or choose $b$.

Thus the full expansion corresponds to all possible ways of making $n$ binary
choices.

The coefficient

$$
\binom{n}{k}
$$

counts the number of ways to choose exactly $k$ of the factors to contribute
$b$.

This is why binomial coefficients appear naturally in combinatorics and
probability.

## Application to Counting

Suppose there are $n$ people, and each person can either be chosen for a team or
not chosen.

There are $2$ choices for each person, so the total number of possible teams is

$$
2^n.
$$

On the other hand, we can count by team size.

There are

$$
\binom{n}{0}
$$

teams of size $0$,

$$
\binom{n}{1}
$$

teams of size $1$,

$$
\binom{n}{2}
$$

teams of size $2$,

and so on.

Therefore,

$$
\binom{n}{0}+\binom{n}{1}+\cdots+\binom{n}{n}
=
2^n.
$$

This is the identity obtained from expanding $(1+1)^n$.

## Application to Probability

The Binomial Theorem is also the foundation of binomial probability.

Suppose an experiment has two possible outcomes
- Success with probability $p$
- Failure with probability $1-p$

If the experiment is repeated $n$ times independently, the probability of
getting exactly $k$ successes is

$$
\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}.
$$

Why?

We must choose which $k$ of the $n$ trials are successes. This can be done in

$$
\binom{n}{k}
$$

ways.

For any fixed set of $k$ successes and $n-k$ failures, the probability is

$$
p^k(1-p)^{n-k}.
$$

Therefore the total probability is

$$
\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}.
$$

If we sum over all possible values of $k$, we get

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}.
$$

By the Binomial Theorem this equals

$$
(p+(1-p))^n=1.
$$

This confirms that the probabilities of all possible numbers of successes sum
to $1$.

## Worked Probability Example

### Example 7

> A fair coin is flipped $6$ times. What is the probability of getting exactly
> $4$ heads?

Here, a success is getting heads, so

$$
p=\frac{1}{2}.
$$

We want exactly $4$ successes in $6$ trials, so

$$
n=6,\qquad k=4.
$$

The probability is

$$
\binom{6}{4}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^4
\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2.
$$

Since

$$
\binom{6}{4}=15,
$$

this becomes

$$
15\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^6
=
\frac{15}{64}.
$$

So the probability is

$$
\frac{15}{64}.
$$

## Key Contest Insight

The Binomial Theorem is useful whenever a problem involves a power of a sum.
Look for expressions such as

$$
(a+b)^n,
$$

$$
(1+x)^n,
$$

or

$$
(m+1)^n.
$$

The theorem can help you:
- Expand powers efficiently
- Find a single coefficient or term
- Prove divisibility statements
- Evaluate binomial sums
- Count subsets
- Compute probabilities

The main idea is that coefficients in expansions count choices.

## Strategy Tips

- Identify the two terms being raised to a power
- Write the general term before expanding everything
- For coefficient problems, solve for the exponent you need
- For constant term problems, set the exponent of the variable equal to $0$
- For modular arithmetic, write numbers as something convenient plus a small
  remainder, such as $101=100+1$
- For combinatorics, remember that $\binom{n}{k}$ counts choices
- For probability, identify the number of successes, the success probability,
  and the number of trials

## Common Pitfalls

- Forgetting the binomial coefficient
- Mixing up $k$ and $n-k$ in the general term
- Losing negative signs in expansions such as $(a-b)^n$
- Expanding the whole expression when only one term is needed
- Forgetting to check whether a term is constant in constant-term problems
- In probability, forgetting to multiply by the number of ways to choose which
  trials are successes
