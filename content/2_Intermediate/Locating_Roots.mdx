## Overview

In many algebra problems, we are not asked to find the exact roots of a
quadratic equation. Instead, we are asked to determine where the roots lie.

For example, a problem might ask:
- Are both roots greater than a certain number?
- Are both roots less than a certain number?
- Is exactly one root inside a given interval?
- Do both roots lie between two numbers?

These questions are called root-location problems.

They are especially common when a quadratic contains a parameter. Instead of
solving for the roots directly, we write inequalities that force the roots to
lie in the desired region.

## Motivation

Consider a quadratic function

$$
f(x)=Ax^2+Bx+C,
$$

where $A\neq 0$.

The roots of the quadratic equation

$$
Ax^2+Bx+C=0
$$

are the points where the graph of $f(x)$ intersects the $x$-axis.

So locating the roots is really a graph question:

> Where does the parabola cross the $x$-axis?

For example, if we want exactly one root between $0$ and $3$, we want the graph
to cross the $x$-axis once inside that interval and once outside it.

Rather than drawing a new graph every time, we can use a few standard
conditions involving:
- The discriminant
- The value of the function at endpoints
- The vertex of the parabola

## Important Notation

Throughout this module, let

$$
f(x)=Ax^2+Bx+C,
$$

where $A\neq 0$.

Let the roots be

$$
\alpha
\quad\text{and}\quad
\beta.
$$

The discriminant is

$$
\Delta=B^2-4AC.
$$

The $x$-coordinate of the vertex is

$$
h=-\frac{B}{2A}.
$$

The sign of $A$ tells us whether the parabola opens upwards or downwards.
- If $A > 0$, the parabola opens upwards
- If $A < 0$, the parabola opens downwards

The expression $Af(k)$ is useful because it compares the sign of the value
$f(k)$ with the direction of the parabola.

## Why the Discriminant Matters

A quadratic has real roots if and only if

$$
\Delta\ge 0.
$$

If

$$
\Delta<0,
$$

then the graph does not cross the $x$-axis at all, so it makes no sense to ask
whether the real roots are greater than or less than some number.

Therefore, whenever we want both roots to be located somewhere, we usually need

$$
\Delta\ge 0.
$$

In some cases, this condition is automatic from the other inequalities, but it
is still useful to understand why it appears.

## Case 1: Both Roots Greater Than $k$

Suppose we want

$$
\alpha>k
\quad\text{and}\quad
\beta>k.
$$

Then both roots lie to the right of $k$.

The required conditions are:

$$
\Delta\ge 0,
$$

$$
Af(k)>0,
$$

and

$$
-\frac{B}{2A}>k.
$$

### Why These Conditions Work

First, we need real roots, so

$$
\Delta\ge 0.
$$

Next, if both roots are to the right of $k$, then the point $x=k$ lies outside
the root interval. At such a point, the graph has the same sign as the leading
coefficient. This is captured by

$$
Af(k)>0.
$$

Finally, the vertex lies halfway between the two roots, so if both roots are
greater than $k$, the vertex must also lie to the right of $k$:

$$
-\frac{B}{2A}>k.
$$

## Case 2: Both Roots Less Than $k$

Suppose we want

$$
\alpha<k
\quad\text{and}\quad
\beta<k.
$$

Then both roots lie to the left of $k$.

The required conditions are:

$$
\Delta\ge 0,
$$

$$
Af(k)>0,
$$

and

$$
-\frac{B}{2A}<k.
$$

This is very similar to the previous case. The only difference is that the
vertex must now lie to the left of $k$.

## Case 3: One Root on Each Side of $k$

Suppose we want one root less than $k$ and the other root greater than $k$:

$$
\alpha<k<\beta.
$$

The required condition is

$$
Af(k)<0.
$$

### Why Only One Condition?

If $A>0$, the parabola opens upwards. Between the two roots, the graph lies
below the $x$-axis, so $f(k)<0$.

If $A<0$, the parabola opens downwards. Between the two roots, the graph lies
above the $x$-axis, so $f(k)>0$.

In both cases, $A$ and $f(k)$ have opposite signs, so

$$
Af(k)<0.
$$

This condition already forces the graph to cross the $x$-axis on both sides of
$k$, so we do not need to separately write

$$
\Delta\ge 0.
$$

## Two-Point Root Location

Now suppose we have two real numbers

$$
k_1<k_2.
$$

Many problems ask us to describe where the roots lie relative to the interval

$$
(k_1,k_2).
$$

There are three especially important cases:
- Both roots lie inside the interval
- Exactly one root lies inside the interval
- One root lies to the left of the interval and the other lies to the right

## Case 4: Both Roots Between $k_1$ and $k_2$

Suppose we want

$$
k_1<\alpha,\beta<k_2.
$$

That means both roots lie inside the interval.

The required conditions are:

$$
\Delta\ge 0,
$$

$$
Af(k_1)>0,
$$

$$
Af(k_2)>0,
$$

and

$$
k_1<-\frac{B}{2A}<k_2.
$$

### Why These Conditions Work

We need real roots, so

$$
\Delta\ge 0.
$$

The endpoints $k_1$ and $k_2$ must both lie outside the root interval. Therefore
the graph at both endpoints must have the same sign as the leading coefficient:

$$
Af(k_1)>0
$$

and

$$
Af(k_2)>0.
$$

Finally, the vertex lies halfway between the two roots. If both roots are inside
the interval, the vertex must also be inside the interval:

$$
k_1<-\frac{B}{2A}<k_2.
$$

All four conditions are needed. If the vertex condition is missing, it is
possible for both roots to lie outside the interval while still having the
correct endpoint signs.

## Case 5: Exactly One Root Between $k_1$ and $k_2$

Suppose we want exactly one root in the interval

$$
(k_1,k_2).
$$

The required condition is

$$
f(k_1)f(k_2)<0.
$$

### Why This Works

If

$$
f(k_1)f(k_2)<0,
$$

then $f(k_1)$ and $f(k_2)$ have opposite signs.

Since a quadratic function is continuous, the graph must cross the $x$-axis
somewhere between $k_1$ and $k_2$.

A quadratic has at most two real roots. If the signs at the two endpoints are
opposite, the number of crossings inside the interval is odd. Therefore, there
is exactly one root in the interval.

This is often the simplest root-location case.

## Case 6: One Root on Each Side of the Interval

Say we want

$$
\alpha<k_1<k_2<\beta.
$$

That means the entire interval lies between the two roots.

The required conditions are:

$$
Af(k_1)<0
$$

and

$$
Af(k_2)<0.
$$

### Why These Conditions Work

If $A>0$, the parabola opens upwards and is negative between its two roots.
As such, if both $f(k_1)$ and $f(k_2)$ are negative, both points lie between the
roots.

If $A<0$, the parabola opens downwards and is positive between its two roots.
As such, if both $f(k_1)$ and $f(k_2)$ are positive, both points again lie between
the roots.

Both situations are captured by

$$
Af(k_1)<0
$$

and

$$
Af(k_2)<0.
$$

This guarantees one root to the left of $k_1$ and the other root to the right of
$k_2$.

## Summary of Conditions

Let

$$
f(x)=Ax^2+Bx+C
$$

with $A\neq 0$, discriminant

$$
\Delta=B^2-4AC,
$$

and vertex $x$-coordinate

$$
h=-\frac{B}{2A}.
$$

### One-Point Cases

Suppose $h=-\frac{B}{2A}$.

#### Both roots greater than $k$

If both roots are greater than $k$, use the conditions

$$
\Delta\ge 0,
$$

$$
Af(k)>0,
$$

and

$$
h>k.
$$

#### Both roots less than $k$

If both roots are less than $k$, use the conditions

$$
\Delta\ge 0,
$$

$$
Af(k)>0,
$$

and

$$
h<k.
$$

#### One root on each side of $k$

If one root is less than $k$ and the other root is greater than $k$, use the
condition

$$
Af(k)<0.
$$

This condition already guarantees that the parabola crosses the $x$-axis on
both sides of $k$.

### Two-Point Cases

Assume

$$
k_1<k_2.
$$

#### Both roots in $(k_1,k_2)$

If both roots lie inside the interval $(k_1,k_2)$, use the conditions

$$
\Delta\ge 0,
$$

$$
Af(k_1)>0,
$$

$$
Af(k_2)>0,
$$

and

$$
k_1<h<k_2.
$$

#### Exactly one root in $(k_1,k_2)$

If exactly one root lies inside the interval $(k_1,k_2)$, use the condition

$$
f(k_1)f(k_2)<0.
$$

This means the function has opposite signs at the two endpoints.

#### One root left of $k_1$, one root right of $k_2$

If one root lies to the left of $k_1$ and the other lies to the right of $k_2$,
use the conditions

$$
Af(k_1)<0
$$

and

$$
Af(k_2)<0.
$$

This means both endpoints lie between the two roots.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1

> Find all real values of $t$ such that both roots of
>
> $$
> x^2-2tx+t+3=0
> $$
>
> are greater than $1$.

Let

$$
f(x)=x^2-2tx+t+3.
$$

Here,

$$
A=1,
\qquad
B=-2t,
\qquad
C=t+3.
$$

We want both roots to be greater than $1$, so we use Case 1 with $k=1$.

First,

$$
\Delta=(-2t)^2-4(1)(t+3)
=
4t^2-4t-12.
$$

We need

$$
4t^2-4t-12\ge 0.
$$

Dividing by $4$ gives

$$
t^2-t-3\ge 0.
$$

Thus

$$
t\le \frac{1-\sqrt{13}}{2}
\quad\text{or}\quad
t\ge \frac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}.
$$

Next,

$$
f(1)=1-2t+t+3=4-t.
$$

Since $A=1$, the condition

$$
Af(1)>0
$$

becomes

$$
4-t>0,
$$

so

$$
t<4.
$$

Finally, the vertex has $x$-coordinate

$$
h=-\frac{B}{2A}=t.
$$

We need

$$
t>1.
$$

Combining all three conditions gives

$$
\frac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\le t<4.
$$

### Example 2

> Find all real values of $c$ such that both roots of
>
> $$
> x^2-5x+c=0
> $$
>
> lie in the interval $(1,4)$.

Let

$$
f(x)=x^2-5x+c.
$$

Here,

$$
A=1,
\qquad
B=-5.
$$

The vertex is

$$
h=-\frac{B}{2A}=\frac{5}{2}.
$$

Since

$$
1<\frac{5}{2}<4,
$$

the vertex condition is already satisfied.

Now compute the discriminant:

$$
\Delta=25-4c.
$$

We need

$$
25-4c\ge 0,
$$

so

$$
c\le \frac{25}{4}.
$$

Next,

$$
f(1)=1-5+c=c-4
$$

and

$$
f(4)=16-20+c=c-4.
$$

Since $A=1$, we need

$$
f(1)>0
$$

and

$$
f(4)>0.
$$

As such

$$
c-4>0,
$$

so

$$
c>4.
$$

Combining the conditions gives

$$
4<c\le \frac{25}{4}.
$$

### Example 3

> Find all real values of $c$ such that exactly one root of
>
> $$
> x^2-5x+c=0
> $$
>
> lies in the interval $(1,4)$.

Let

$$
f(x)=x^2-5x+c.
$$

For exactly one root in $(1,4)$, use Case 5:

$$
f(1)f(4)<0.
$$

But

$$
f(1)=c-4
$$

and

$$
f(4)=c-4.
$$

As such

$$
f(1)f(4)=(c-4)^2.
$$

This can never be negative.

Therefore, there are no real values of $c$ for which exactly one root lies in
$(1,4)$.

This makes sense because the quadratic

$$
x^2-5x+c
$$

has axis of symmetry

$$
x=\frac{5}{2},
$$

which is exactly in the middle of the interval. Its roots are symmetric around
the centre of the interval, so they enter or leave the interval together.

### Example 4

> Find all real values of $c$ such that one root of
>
> $$
> x^2-5x+c=0
> $$
>
> lies to the left of $1$ and the other lies to the right of $4$.

We want

$$
\alpha<1<4<\beta.
$$

So we use Case 6:

$$
Af(1)<0
$$

and

$$
Af(4)<0.
$$

Since $A=1$, this becomes

$$
f(1)<0
$$

and

$$
f(4)<0.
$$

Now

$$
f(1)=c-4
$$

and

$$
f(4)=c-4.
$$

Therefore we need

$$
c-4<0.
$$

So

$$
c<4.
$$

Therefore, for every

$$
c<4,
$$

one root lies to the left of $1$ and the other lies to the right of $4$.

## At Least One Root in an Interval

Sometimes a problem asks for at least one root in

$$
(k_1,k_2).
$$

This is not a single new case. It is the union of two possibilities:
- Exactly one root lies in the interval
- Both roots lie in the interval

So you should combine Case 5 and Case 4.

In contest problems, it is often easier to solve these two cases separately and
then take the union of the answer sets.

## Key Contest Insight

Root-location problems are graph problems disguised as algebra problems.

The conditions are not random inequalities. Each one has a geometric meaning:
- $\Delta\ge 0$ ensures real roots exist.
- $Af(k)>0$ means $k$ lies outside the interval between the roots.
- $Af(k)<0$ means $k$ lies between the roots.
- $-\frac{B}{2A}$ gives the horizontal position of the vertex.

Once these ideas are clear, the cases become much easier to remember.

## Strategy Tips
- Start by writing down $f(x)$, $A$, $B$, $C$, and $\Delta$
- Compute the vertex coordinate $h=-\frac{B}{2A}$
- Decide which root-location case matches the problem
- Write the required inequalities carefully
- Solve all inequalities and take their common solution
- Be careful with strict intervals such as $(k_1,k_2)$
- If the problem says 'at least one root', split it into cases

## Common Pitfalls
- Forgetting to check the discriminant when both roots are required
- Using $f(k)>0$ instead of $Af(k)>0$ when the parabola may open downwards
- Forgetting that the vertex lies midway between the two roots
- Combining endpoint conditions incorrectly
- Treating 'exactly one root in an interval' the same as 'both roots in an interval'
- Ignoring whether the interval is open or closed
