## Overview

Polar form turns multiplication into angle addition and scaling. It is the
fastest path for high powers.

## Core Skills

### Convert to Polar Form

Compute $r=|z|$ and $\theta=\arg z$ so $z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)$.

### Multiply and Divide

Multiply magnitudes and add arguments; divide magnitudes and subtract
arguments.

### Reduce Angles

Always reduce angles modulo $2\pi$ to keep arguments in a standard range.

## Key Ideas

- $z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = re^{i\theta}$.
- De Moivre: $z^n = r^n(\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta)$.
- Multiply by adding arguments and multiplying magnitudes.

## Worked Example

Compute $(1+i)^{10}$.

$1+i = \sqrt{2}e^{i\pi/4}$. Then
$(1+i)^{10} = (\sqrt{2})^{10} e^{i\cdot 10\pi/4} = 2^5 e^{i\pi/2} = 32i$.

## More Examples
## Example 1: Power

Compute $(2e^{i\pi/3})^4$.

$2^4e^{i4\pi/3}=16\left(\cos\frac{4\pi}{3}+i\sin\frac{4\pi}{3}\right)$.

## Example 2: Product

If $z_1=3e^{i\pi/6}$ and $z_2=2e^{i\pi/4}$, find $z_1z_2$.

$6e^{i(\pi/6+\pi/4)}=6e^{i5\pi/12}$.

## Example 3: Division

Compute $\frac{4e^{i\pi/2}}{2e^{i\pi/3}}$.

$2e^{i(\pi/2-\pi/3)}=2e^{i\pi/6}$.

## Example 4: Interesting 5th roots

Take the expression $z_k=\left(2\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{6}\right)^{1/5}$ represent the five fifth roots of $2\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{6}$.

Your goal is to compute $\prod_{k=0}^{4}z_k^2$.

To start off, the product of all fifth roots just equals the original complex number itself:

$\prod_{k=0}^{4}z_k=2\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{6}$.

Consequently, it follows that

$\prod_{k=0}^{4}z_k^2=\left(2\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{6}\right)^2=4\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{3}$.

## Example 5: When does it become imaginary? (It is advised to fully understand and remember this type of problem/solution!)

Assume that $z=\sqrt3+i$.

Your job is to find the smallest positive integer $n$ such that the following expression is purely imaginary: $z^n$ .

After that, we can simply turn it to polar form:

$z=2\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{6}$.

Next,

$z^n=2^n\mathrm{cis}\frac{n\pi}{6}$.

Now, observe that for $z^n$ to be purely imaginary,

$\cos\frac{n\pi}{6}=0$.

As a result, we have that

$\frac{n\pi}{6}=\frac{\pi}{2}+k\pi$.

So, we get our answer:

$n=3+6k$.

The smallest positive integer is $\boxed{3}$.

## Example 6: Symmetry!

Let $z=\mathrm{cis}\theta$.

Given that $z^4+z^{-4}=0$, you must find ALL possible values of $\theta$ for $0<\theta<\pi$.

We can first approach this problem by utilizing De Moivre, which results in:

$z^4=\mathrm{cis}(4\theta)$ and $z^{-4}=\mathrm{cis}(-4\theta)$.

Then, combining them gives

$z^4+z^{-4}=2\cos(4\theta)$.

And consequently,

$2\cos(4\theta)=0$,

which means

$\cos(4\theta)=0$.

So we have:

$4\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}+k\pi$.

And from here we can easily obtain our answers:

$\theta=\frac{\pi}{8}+\frac{k\pi}{4}$.

The values in $(0,\pi)$ are

$\frac{\pi}{8},\frac{3\pi}{8},\frac{5\pi}{8},\frac{7\pi}{8}$.

## Example 7: Binomial Expressions! (This you also need to fully understand and remember as this trick is very useful!)

Your job is to compute $(1+i)^{12}$.

At first, the expression looks daunting, but we can tackle it using the techniques we learned in this module. First, we turn $1+i$ into polar form:

$1+i=\sqrt2\mathrm{cis}\frac{\pi}{4}$.

Then, we can do the following:

$(1+i)^{12}=(\sqrt2)^{12}\mathrm{cis}\left(12\cdot\frac{\pi}{4}\right)$.

Simplifying the expression, we get,

$=64\mathrm{cis}(3\pi)$.

Because $\cos3\pi=-1$ and $\sin3\pi=0$, we get our answer:

$(1+i)^{12}=-64$.

Note: Can you interpret this problem geometrically? Think out of the box!

## Example 8: Roots of Unity + Polar Form!

Let $\omega=\mathrm{cis}\frac{2\pi}{9}$.

Your job is to compute the following expression: $(\omega+\omega^8)^2+(\omega^2+\omega^7)^2+(\omega^4+\omega^5)^2$.

First off, because $\omega^8=\omega^{-1}$, $\omega^7=\omega^{-2}$, and $\omega^5=\omega^{-4}$, we can use

$\omega^k+\omega^{-k}=2\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{9}\right)$.

From here, the expression simply becomes

$4\cos^2\frac{2\pi}{9}+4\cos^2\frac{4\pi}{9}+4\cos^2\frac{8\pi}{9}$.

And using

$\cos^2x=\frac{1+\cos(2x)}{2}$,

this just simplifies to

$2\left(3+\cos\frac{2\pi}{9}+\cos\frac{4\pi}{9}+\cos\frac{8\pi}{9}\right)$.

Then, using the following identity

$\cos\frac{2\pi}{9}+\cos\frac{4\pi}{9}+\cos\frac{8\pi}{9}=0$,

we get that the final value is $6$.


## Strategy Checklist

- Convert to polar form before powering.
- Add or subtract arguments correctly.
- Reduce angles to a standard range.

## Common Pitfalls

- Using degrees in one step and radians in another.
- Forgetting to reduce angles modulo $2\pi$.
- Mixing up magnitude and argument operations.
