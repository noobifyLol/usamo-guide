## Introduction

When we first learn about square roots, a natural question comes up: what is $\sqrt{-1}$? If your teacher told you it doesn't exist, they were ~~completely wrong~~ both right and wrong at the same time. It doesn't exist as a *real* number. But mathematicians decided that was a perfectly good reason to *invent* a new kind of number.

We define the **imaginary unit** $i$ by the equation

$$
i^2 = -1, \quad \text{equivalently} \quad i = \sqrt{-1}
$$

Numbers formed by multiplying $i$ by a real number — like $2i$, $-5i$, or $\pi i$ — are called **pure imaginary numbers**. They cannot be simplified any further; $2i$ is already in its simplest form.

> **Warning**: The rule $\sqrt{a}\sqrt{b} = \sqrt{ab}$ breaks down for negative numbers. For example,
>
> $$
> \sqrt{-1}\sqrt{-1} \neq \sqrt{(-1)^2} = 1
> $$
>
> The correct answer is
>
> $$
> i \cdot i = i^2 = -1
> $$
>
> Never combine square roots of negative numbers under one radical.

A **complex number** is any number of the form

$$
z = a + bi, \qquad a,b \in \mathbb{R}, \qquad i^2 = -1
$$

The set of all complex numbers is denoted $\mathbb{C}$. This is a strict superset: every real number is a complex number (take $b=0$), and every pure imaginary number is a complex number (take $a=0$).

For a complex number $z = a + bi$:

- $\operatorname{Re}(z)=a$ is the **real part**
- $\operatorname{Im}(z)=b$ is the **imaginary part**

Note that $b$ itself is real — it is the coefficient of $i$.

So,

$$
\operatorname{Re}(3-7i)=3
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\operatorname{Im}(3-7i)=-7
$$

not $-7i$.

## Powers of $i$

If we keep multiplying $i$ by itself, we get a cycle that repeats every $4$ steps:

$$
i^1=i,\qquad
i^2=-1,\qquad
i^3=-i,\qquad
i^4=1,\qquad
i^5=i,\ldots
$$

The key rule is:

$$
i^n = i^(n mod 4)
$$

where the remainder is taken in $\{0,1,2,3\}$ and $i^0=1$.

> **2019 AMC 10A Problem 4**
>
> What is $i^{2019}$?
>
> Since
>
> $$
> 2019 = 4 \cdot 504 + 3
> $$
>
> we get
>
> $$
> i^{2019}=i^3=\boxed{-i}
> $$

The mod-$4$ trick is mechanical once you see it — and it appears on AMC/AIME more often than you'd expect.

## Algebra of Complex Numbers

### Addition and Subtraction

Add or subtract real and imaginary parts separately:

$$
(a+bi)\pm(c+di)
=
(a\pm c)+(b\pm d)i
$$

This is exactly like vector addition componentwise.

### Multiplication

Expand using FOIL and replace $i^2$ with $-1$:

$$
(a+bi)(c+di)
=
ac+adi+bci+bdi^2
=
(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)i
$$

You do not need to memorize this formula.

**Example:** Compute $(2+3i)(1-4i)$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
(2+3i)(1-4i)
&=2(1)+2(-4i)+3i(1)+3i(-4i) \\
&=2-8i+3i-12i^2 \\
&=2-5i+12 \\
&=14-5i
\end{aligned}
$$

### Equating Real and Imaginary Parts

Two complex numbers are equal iff their real parts and imaginary parts are equal:

$$
a+bi=c+di
\iff
a=c \text{ and } b=d
$$

This means one complex equation is secretly two real equations.

**Example:** Find real $x,y$ such that

$$
(x+yi)(2+3i)=1+13i
$$

Expanding:

$$
(2x-3y)+(3x+2y)i
$$

Equating parts:

$$
2x-3y=1
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
3x+2y=13
$$

Multiply the first equation by $2$ and the second by $3$:

$$
4x-6y=2
$$

$$
9x+6y=39
$$

Adding:

$$
13x=41
\implies
x=\frac{41}{13}
$$

Substituting back:

$$
y=-\frac{23}{13}
$$

## Conjugates

The **conjugate** of

$$
z=a+bi
$$

is

$$
\overline{z}=a-bi
$$

Geometrically, this reflects the point across the real axis.

The key identity is:

$$
z\overline{z}
=
(a+bi)(a-bi)
=
a^2+b^2
$$

The product is always a nonnegative real number.

Two useful identities:

$$
z+\overline{z}=2\operatorname{Re}(z)
$$

$$
z-\overline{z}=2i\operatorname{Im}(z)
$$

Properties of conjugation:

$$
\overline{z+w}=\overline{z}+\overline{w}
$$

$$
\overline{zw}=\overline{z}\,\overline{w}
$$

## Division

To divide complex numbers, multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator.

$$
\frac{1}{a+bi}
=
\frac{a-bi}{a^2+b^2}
$$

More generally,

$$
\frac{a+bi}{c+di}
=
\frac{(a+bi)(c-di)}{c^2+d^2}
$$

**Example:** Compute

$$
\frac{3+2*i}{1-4*i}
$$

Multiply top and bottom by $1+4i$:

$$
\frac{3+2*i}{1-4*i}
\cdot
\frac{1+4*i}{1+4*i}
=
\frac{(3+2*i)(1+4*i)}{1^2+4^2}
$$

Expand the numerator:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(3+2i)(1+4i)
&=3+12i+2i+8i^2 \\
&=3+14i-8 \\
&=-5+14i
\end{aligned}
$$

Denominator:

$$
1+16=17
$$

Therefore,

$$
\frac{3+2*i}{1-4*i}
=
\frac{-5+14*i}{17}
=
-\frac{5}{17}+\frac{14}{17}i
$$

## Worked Examples

> **Example 1:** Compute $i^{2023}$.
>
> Since
>
> $$
> 2023=4\cdot505+3
> $$
>
> we get
>
> $$
> i^{2023}=i^3=-i
> $$

> **Example 2:** If $z+6i=iz$, find $z$.
>
> Rearranging:
>
> $$
> z-iz=-6i
> $$
>
> $$
> z(1-i)=-6i
> $$
>
> Thus,
>
> $$
> z
> =
> \frac{-6*i}{1-i}
> \cdot
> \frac{1+i}{1+i}
> =
> \frac{-6*i(1+i)}{2}
> =
> \frac{-6*i+6}{2}
> =
> 3-3i
> $$

> **Example 3:** Let
>
> $$
> z+\overline{z}=6
> $$
>
> and
>
> $$
> z\overline{z}=13
> $$
>
> Find $z$.
>
> Write
>
> $$
> z=a+bi
> $$
>
> Then
>
> $$
> 2a=6
> \implies
> a=3
> $$
>
> Also,
>
> $$
> a^2+b^2=13
> $$
>
> so
>
> $$
> 9+b^2=13
> \implies
> b^2=4
> \implies
> b=\pm2
> $$
>
> Therefore,
>
> $$
> z=3+2i
> \quad\text{or}\quad
> z=3-2i
> $$

> **Example 4 (AIME flavor):** Find all complex $z$ such that
>
> $$
> z^2=2i
> $$
>
> Write
>
> $$
> z=a+bi
> $$
>
> Then
>
> $$
> z^2=(a^2-b^2)+2abi
> $$
>
> Equating parts:
>
> $$
> a^2-b^2=0
> $$
>
> $$
> 2ab=2
> \implies
> ab=1
> $$
>
> Since $ab>0$, we must have $a=b$.
>
> Then
>
> $$
> a^2=1
> $$
>
> giving
>
> $$
> z=1+i
> \quad\text{or}\quad
> z=-1-i
> $$

## Strategy Checklist

| Task | Technique |
|---|---|
| Simplify $i^n$ | Compute $n \bmod 4$ |
| Solve for complex variables | Expand and equate real/imaginary parts |
| Eliminate $i$ from denominator | Multiply by conjugate |
| Extract $\operatorname{Re}(z)$ or $\operatorname{Im}(z)$ | Use conjugate identities |
| Relate $z$ and $\overline{z}$ | Use $z\overline{z}=a^2+b^2$ |
| Solve equations like $z^2=w$ | Write $z=a+bi$ and equate parts |

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating $\operatorname{Im}(z)$ as $bi$ instead of $b$
- Forgetting that $i^2=-1$
- Incorrectly combining square roots of negative numbers
- Confusing $z+\overline{z}$ with $2|z|$
- Forgetting to multiply numerator *and* denominator by the conjugate
- Changing the sign of the real part when conjugating

## Remarks

Everything in this module has been purely algebraic. But there is a beautiful geometric picture underneath all of it. In the next module, we interpret

$$
z=a+bi
$$

as the point $(a,b)$ in the plane and discover that conjugation is reflection, addition is vector addition, and multiplication becomes rotation and scaling.

Seeing the geometry makes many of the identities here feel ~~obvious~~ almost inevitable.
