## Overview

Trigonometric identities are some of the most useful tools in olympiad
trigonometry. They allow us to transform complicated expressions into simpler
ones.

These techniques are especially useful when expressions contain products of
sines and cosines, repeated angles, or long sums involving trigonometric terms.

The main goal is not to memorise identities blindly, but to understand when and
why each identity is useful.

## Motivation

Consider the expression

$$
\sin 20^\circ \cos 40^\circ.
$$

At first, this product does not look easy to simplify. However, product to sum
identities allow us to rewrite it as a sum:

$$
\sin A \cos B
=
\frac{1}{2}(\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)).
$$

Using $A=20^\circ$ and $B=40^\circ$, we get

$$
\sin 20^\circ \cos 40^\circ
=
\frac{1}{2}(\sin 60^\circ+\sin(-20^\circ)).
$$

Since $\sin(-20^\circ)=-\sin 20^\circ$, this becomes

$$
\frac{1}{2}(\sin 60^\circ-\sin 20^\circ).
$$

A product has been changed into a sum, which is often easier to combine with
other terms.

This is the central idea behind many trigonometric identities, rewrite an
expression into a form where cancellation, symmetry, or known values become
visible.

## Angle Addition Formulas

Most of the identities in this module come from the angle addition formulas.

For all angles $A$ and $B$,

$$
\sin(A+B)=\sin A\cos B+\cos A\sin B
$$

and

$$
\sin(A-B)=\sin A\cos B-\cos A\sin B.
$$

Also,

$$
\cos(A+B)=\cos A\cos B-\sin A\sin B
$$

and

$$
\cos(A-B)=\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B.
$$

These four formulas are the foundation for product to sum, sum to product,
double angle, and triple angle identities.

## Product to Sum Identities

Product to sum identities rewrite products of trigonometric functions as sums.

They are especially useful when several product terms appear together.

### Main Identities

$$
\sin A\cos B
=
\frac{1}{2}(\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B))
$$

$$
\cos A\sin B
=
\frac{1}{2}(\sin(A+B)-\sin(A-B))
$$

$$
\cos A\cos B
=
\frac{1}{2}(\cos(A+B)+\cos(A-B))
$$

$$
\sin A\sin B
=
\frac{1}{2}(\cos(A-B)-\cos(A+B)).
$$

These identities come directly from adding or subtracting the angle addition
formulas.

## Deriving a Product to Sum Identity

Let us derive one example.

From the sine addition formulas,

$$
\sin(A+B)=\sin A\cos B+\cos A\sin B
$$

and

$$
\sin(A-B)=\sin A\cos B-\cos A\sin B.
$$

Adding these equations gives

$$
\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)=2\sin A\cos B.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sin A\cos B
=
\frac{1}{2}(\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)).
$$

The other product to sum identities are derived similarly.

## Sum to Product Identities

The same identities can be read in reverse. This gives sum to product
identities.

These are useful when a problem contains sums such as $\sin A+\sin B$ or
$\cos A-\cos B$.

### Main Identities

$$
\sin X+\sin Y
=
2\sin\left(\frac{X+Y}{2}\right)
\cos\left(\frac{X-Y}{2}\right)
$$

$$
\sin X-\sin Y
=
2\cos\left(\frac{X+Y}{2}\right)
\sin\left(\frac{X-Y}{2}\right)
$$

$$
\cos X+\cos Y
=
2\cos\left(\frac{X+Y}{2}\right)
\cos\left(\frac{X-Y}{2}\right)
$$

$$
\cos X-\cos Y
=
-2\sin\left(\frac{X+Y}{2}\right)
\sin\left(\frac{X-Y}{2}\right).
$$

These often appear in problems involving long sums or equations where
factorisation is needed.

## Double angle Identities

Double angle identities express trigonometric functions of $2x$ in terms of
functions of $x$.

### Sine Double angle Formula

Using

$$
\sin(A+B)=\sin A\cos B+\cos A\sin B,
$$

and setting $A=B=x$, we get

$$
\sin 2x=2\sin x\cos x.
$$

### Cosine Double angle Formula

Using

$$
\cos(A+B)=\cos A\cos B-\sin A\sin B,
$$

and setting $A=B=x$, we get

$$
\cos 2x=\cos^2 x-\sin^2 x.
$$

Using

$$
\sin^2 x+\cos^2 x=1,
$$

we also get the equivalent forms

$$
\cos 2x=2\cos^2 x-1
$$

and

$$
\cos 2x=1-2\sin^2 x.
$$

### Tangent Double angle Formula

Since

$$
\tan 2x=\frac{\sin 2x}{\cos 2x},
$$

we get

$$
\tan 2x
=
\frac{2\tan x}{1-\tan^2 x},
$$

provided the expressions are defined.

## Auxiliary Angle Method

The auxiliary angle method is used to rewrite expressions of the form

$$
a\sin x+b\cos x
$$

as a single sine or cosine expression.

The key idea is to write

$$
a\sin x+b\cos x=R\sin(x+\phi).
$$

Using the angle addition formula,

$$
R\sin(x+\phi)
=
R\sin x\cos\phi+R\cos x\sin\phi.
$$

Comparing coefficients with

$$
a\sin x+b\cos x,
$$

we need

$$
R\cos\phi=a
$$

and

$$
R\sin\phi=b.
$$

Squaring and adding gives

$$
R^2=a^2+b^2,
$$

so

$$
R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}.
$$

Also,

$$
\tan\phi=\frac{b}{a},
$$

provided $a\neq 0$.

Therefore,

$$
a\sin x+b\cos x
=
R\sin(x+\phi),
$$

where

$$
R=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}.
$$

Since the maximum value of $\sin(x+\phi)$ is $1$ and the minimum value is $-1$,
the maximum value of

$$
a\sin x+b\cos x
$$

is

$$
R
$$

and the minimum value is

$$
-R.
$$

### Example

> Find the maximum and minimum values of
>
> $$
> 3\sin x+4\cos x.
> $$

Here,

$$
a=3
$$

and

$$
b=4.
$$

As such

$$
R=\sqrt{3^2+4^2}=5.
$$

So

$$
3\sin x+4\cos x
$$

has maximum value $5$ and minimum value $-5$.

We can also write it as

$$
3\sin x+4\cos x=5\sin(x+\phi),
$$

where

$$
\cos\phi=\frac{3}{5}
$$

and

$$
\sin\phi=\frac{4}{5}.
$$
