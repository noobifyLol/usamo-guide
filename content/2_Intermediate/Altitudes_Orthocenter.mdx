# Altitudes and the Orthocenter


## Overview

Altitudes are perpendicular segments drawn from a vertex to the opposite side (or its extension). They play a central role in triangle geometry because they connect directly to area and often create right triangles, similar triangles, cyclic quadrilaterals, and useful length relationships.

The three altitudes of a triangle intersect at a single point called the orthocenter. The location of the orthocenter depends entirely on the type of triangle.

## Definitions and Key Ideas

An *altitude* of a triangle is a line through a vertex perpendicular to the opposite side (or its extension).

It can be proven that the three altitudes of a triangle intersect at one point; this is called the *orthocenter.*

- In a triangle, area $K = \frac{1}{2}ah_a$ (etc.), so $h_a=\frac{2K}(a}, h_b=\frac{2K}(b},  h_c=\frac{2K}(c}.$
- In a triangle, $\frac{h_a}{hb}=\frac{b}(a},$ and similarly for any pair of altitudes.
- Multiple equal angles and similar triangles are produced by the altitudes, found via angle chasing and cyclic quadrilaterals.
- Quadrilaterals such as $A H_C H H_B$ and $B H_C H_B C$ are cyclic.

- In a right triangle, the following properties hold:
$$
CD^2=AD\cdot DB,
$$

$$
AC^2=AB\cdot AD,
$$

$$
BC^2=AB\cdot DB.
$$

- In an acute triangle, the orthocenter is inside the triangle.
- In a right triangle, the orthocenter is the right-angle vertex.
- In an obtuse triangle, the orthocenter lies outside the triangle.


## Properties
Let $\triangle ABC$ have side lengths $a, b, c$ opposite vertices $A,B,C,$ with corresponding altitudes $h_a, h_b, h_c.$ Let the feet of the altitudes be $H_A, H_B, H_C.$

### Relationship to Area
Because any side may be chosen as a base for a triangle, the area $K$ satisfies

$$K=\frac12 ah_a=\frac12 bh_b=\frac12 ch_c.$$

*Corollary 1:* $h_a=\frac{2K}(a}, h_b=\frac{2K}(b},  h_c=\frac{2K}(c}$

*Corollary 2:* $ah_a=bh_b=ch_c=2K,$ and so

$$\frac{h_a}{h_b}=\frac{b}(a},$$

and similarly for any pair of altitudes.

### Right Triangle Altitudes


Dropping an altitude often creates similar triangles, and one especially important case occurs in a right triangle. Suppose $ABC$ is a right triangle with right angle at $C$, and altitude $CD$ is drawn from $C$ to hypotenuse $AB$.

Then

$$
\triangle ABC \sim \triangle ACD \sim \triangle CBD.
$$

This leads to several useful relationships:

$$
CD^2=AD\cdot DB,
$$

$$
AC^2=AB\cdot AD,
$$

$$
BC^2=AB\cdot DB.
$$

These formulas appear regularly in AMC geometry problems.

*Connection:* These relationships can also be proven using Power of a Point. Considering the first equation, reflect $C$ across hypotenuse $AB$ to point $C',$ to yield a cyclic quadrilateral $AC' B C.$ Then $(CD)(C'D) = (AD)(DB),$ and the proof follows from how $C'D=CD.$ Similarly, reflect $D$ across $AC$ to $D'$ and consider cyclic quadrilateral $AD'CD.$ It will have diameter $AC,$ so we know $BC$ is a tangent to its circumcircle, since $BC \perp AC.$ Then use the tangent/secant statement of PoAP to obtain $BC^2 = BD \cdot BA.$ The proof of the second statement is similar.

### Angle Chasing and Cyclic Quadrilaterals

We'll use $\angle A$ to refer to the largest angle $\angle BAC,$ and so on. It holds that

$$\angle H_C H H_B = \angle B H C = 180 - \angle A,$$

and likewise for the other two vertices.


*Proof:* Use $180 - \angle H_B H C$ or $360 - (\angle A + \angle A H_C C + \angle A H_B H).$ Or, use the fact that $A H_C H H_B$ is cyclic!


Notice that $A H_C H H_B$ is cyclic, and so is $CH_B H H_A $ and $B H_A H H_C,$ since opposite angles sum to $180^\circ.$ Let's only angle chase on $A H_C H H_B$ and
generalize by symmetry. Angles that subtend the same arc are equal, so $\angle H_C H_B H = \angle H_C A H = 90 - \angle B,$ and $\angle H H_C H_B = \angle HA H_B = 90 -
\angle C.$ Similarly, $\angle A H H_B = \angle A H_C H_B = \angle C,$ and $\angle A H H_C = \angle A H_B H_C = \angle B.$

*Note:* The quadrilaterals $BH_C H_B C, A H_C H_A C, A H_B H_A B$ are also cyclic, since opposite angles formed by a diagonal and side are equal. Circumcircles of two cyclic quadrilaterals have been drawn in the below diagram to help visualize this.

Configurations involving the feet of altitudes and the orthocenter frequently lead to cyclic quadrilaterals and angle-chasing opportunities. Whenever you see multiple altitudes, look for right angles and consider whether four points lie on a common circle.

Triangle $\triangle H_A H_B H_C$ is called the **orthic triangle** of $\triangle ABC,$ and the diagram reveals a really nice property: the altitudes of $\triangle ABC$ are the *angle bisectors* of the orthic triangle! Also, the interior angles of the orthic triangle are

$$180-2\angle A, 180-2\angle B, 180-2\angle C,$$

which indeed sum to $540 - 2(\angle A+\angle B+\angle C) = 180^\circ.$


### Location
Up until now, we have been considering an acute triangle. What happens if the triangle is right, or obtuse?

- In an acute triangle, the orthocenter is inside the triangle.
- In a right triangle, the orthocenter is the right-angle vertex.
- In an obtuse triangle, the orthocenter lies outside the triangle.


In an acute triangle, all three altitudes lie inside the triangle. Therefore, the orthocenter also lies inside the triangle.

If $\angle C=90^\circ$, then side $AC$ is perpendicular to $BC$. Since each leg already serves as an altitude, the orthocenter is the right-angle vertex itself.

When a triangle contains an angle greater than $90^\circ$, some altitudes intersect extensions of the sides rather than the sides themselves. As a result, the orthocenter lies outside the triangle.



## Worked Example

Triangle $ABC$ has area $24$ and base $BC=8$. Find the altitude from $A$.

Using

$$
A=\frac{1}{2}bh,
$$

we get

$$
24=\frac{1}{2}(8)h.
$$

Thus

$$
24=4h,
$$

so

$$
h=6.
$$

## More Examples

### Example 1: Right Triangle Orthocenter

In a right triangle $ABC$ with right angle at $C$, where is the orthocenter?

Since the legs are already altitudes, the orthocenter is

$$
\boxed{C}.
$$

### Example 2: Area and Alternate Base

Triangle $ABC$ has area $30$ and side $AC=10$. Find the altitude from $B$.

Using

$$
A=\frac{1}{2}bh,
$$

we obtain

$$
30=\frac{1}{2}(10)h.
$$

Thus

$$
h=6.
$$

### Example 3: Orthocenter Location

A triangle has angles

$$
40^\circ,\quad 50^\circ,\quad 90^\circ.
$$

Where is its orthocenter?

Because the triangle is right, the orthocenter lies at the right-angle vertex.

### Example 4: Altitude to the Hypotenuse

In a right triangle, the altitude to the hypotenuse divides the hypotenuse into segments of lengths $4$ and $9$. Find the altitude.

Using

$$
CD^2=AD\cdot DB,
$$

we get

$$
CD^2=(4)(9)=36.
$$

Therefore,

$$
CD=6.
$$

### Example 5: Triangle DIH

Let $ABC$ be an acute triangle with orthocenter $H$. Let $D$ and $I$ be the feet of the altitudes from $A$ and $B$, respectively.

Find $\angle DIH$.

Since $I$ lies on altitude $BI$, we have

$$
BI\perp AC.
$$

Also, $HD$ lies on altitude $AD$, so

$$
HD\perp BC.
$$

Because $DI$ lies along the side connecting two feet of altitudes, angle chasing shows that $D$, $I$, $B$, and $H$ lie on a common circle.

Therefore,

$$
\angle DIH=\angle DBH=90^\circ.
$$

Thus,

$$
\boxed{\angle DIH=90^\circ}.
$$

## Strategy Checklist

* Mark which segment is the base for the altitude you need.
* Use $A=\tfrac{1}{2}bh$ before introducing extra variables.
* Decide whether the triangle is acute, right, or obtuse before placing the orthocenter.
* Look for right triangles created by altitudes.
* Check for similar triangles whenever an altitude is dropped.
* In right triangles, remember the altitude-to-hypotenuse relationships.
* When multiple altitudes appear, search for cyclic quadrilaterals.

## Common Pitfalls

* Forgetting the factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ in the area formula.
* Confusing an altitude with a median.
* Using the wrong base for a given altitude.
* Assuming the orthocenter is always inside the triangle.
* Missing similar triangles created by an altitude.
* Forgetting that an altitude may land on an extension of a side in an obtuse triangle.
* Overlooking cyclic quadrilaterals formed by altitude feet and the orthocenter.
