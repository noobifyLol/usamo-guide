# Altitudes and the Orthocenter

Author: USAMO Guide Team

## Overview

Altitudes are perpendicular segments drawn from a vertex to the opposite side (or its extension). They play a central role in triangle geometry because they connect directly to area and often create right triangles, similar triangles, and useful length relationships.

The three altitudes of a triangle intersect at a single point called the orthocenter. The location of the orthocenter depends entirely on the type of triangle.

## Definitions and Key Ideas

In triangle $ABC$, let the altitude from $A$ meet $BC$ at $D$.

By definition,

$$
AD \perp BC.
$$

The area of a triangle can be written as

$$
A=\frac{1}{2}bh.
$$

If side $a$ is chosen as the base and $h_a$ is the corresponding altitude, then

$$
A=\frac{1}{2}ah_a.
$$

Solving for the altitude gives

$$
h_a=\frac{2A}{a}.
$$

The three altitudes of a triangle always intersect at a single point called the orthocenter.

## The Orthocenter

The orthocenter behaves differently depending on the type of triangle.

### Acute Triangles

In an acute triangle, all three altitudes lie inside the triangle. Therefore, the orthocenter also lies inside the triangle.

### Right Triangles

If $\angle C=90^\circ$, then side $AC$ is perpendicular to $BC$. Since each leg already serves as an altitude, the orthocenter is the right-angle vertex itself:

$$
H=C.
$$

### Obtuse Triangles

When a triangle contains an angle greater than $90^\circ$, some altitudes intersect extensions of the sides rather than the sides themselves. As a result, the orthocenter lies outside the triangle.

A useful summary is:

* Acute $\rightarrow$ orthocenter inside the triangle.
* Right $\rightarrow$ orthocenter at the right-angle vertex.
* Obtuse $\rightarrow$ orthocenter outside the triangle.

## Area and Altitudes

The most common AMC use of altitudes is area.

If the area and a base are known, the corresponding altitude is immediately determined:

$$
h=\frac{2A}{b}.
$$

Likewise, if two different bases are known, area can connect multiple altitudes.

For example, if

$$
A=30
$$

and

$$
AB=12,
$$

then the altitude to side $AB$ is

$$
h=\frac{2(30)}{12}=5.
$$

## Similar Triangles from Altitudes

Dropping an altitude often creates similar triangles.

One especially important case occurs in a right triangle.

Suppose $ABC$ is a right triangle with right angle at $C$, and altitude $CD$ is drawn from $C$ to hypotenuse $AB$.

Then

$$
\triangle ABC \sim \triangle ACD \sim \triangle CBD.
$$

This leads to several useful relationships:

$$
CD^2=AD\cdot DB,
$$

$$
AC^2=AB\cdot AD,
$$

and

$$
BC^2=AB\cdot DB.
$$

These formulas appear regularly in AMC geometry problems.

## Triangle DIH

A particularly useful orthocenter configuration involves triangle $DIH$.

Let $ABC$ be an acute triangle with orthocenter $H$. Let the altitude from $A$ meet $BC$ at $D$, and let $I$ be the foot of the altitude from $B$ onto $AC$.

Since

$$
HD\perp BC
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
DI\perp AC,
$$

while

$$
HI\perp AC,
$$

we have

$$
\angle DIH=90^\circ.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\angle IDH=90^\circ.
$$

Therefore, quadrilateral $DIBH$ is cyclic.

Configurations involving the feet of altitudes and the orthocenter frequently lead to cyclic quadrilaterals and angle-chasing opportunities. Whenever you see multiple altitudes, look for right angles and consider whether four points lie on a common circle.

## Worked Example

Triangle $ABC$ has area $24$ and base $BC=8$. Find the altitude from $A$.

Using

$$
A=\frac{1}{2}bh,
$$

we get

$$
24=\frac{1}{2}(8)h.
$$

Thus

$$
24=4h,
$$

so

$$
h=6.
$$

## More Examples

### Example 1: Right Triangle Orthocenter

In a right triangle $ABC$ with right angle at $C$, where is the orthocenter?

Since the legs are already altitudes, the orthocenter is

$$
\boxed{C}.
$$

### Example 2: Area and Alternate Base

Triangle $ABC$ has area $30$ and side $AC=10$. Find the altitude from $B$.

Using

$$
A=\frac{1}{2}bh,
$$

we obtain

$$
30=\frac{1}{2}(10)h.
$$

Thus

$$
h=6.
$$

### Example 3: Orthocenter Location

A triangle has angles

$$
40^\circ,\quad 50^\circ,\quad 90^\circ.
$$

Where is its orthocenter?

Because the triangle is right, the orthocenter lies at the right-angle vertex.

### Example 4: Altitude to the Hypotenuse

In a right triangle, the altitude to the hypotenuse divides the hypotenuse into segments of lengths $4$ and $9$. Find the altitude.

Using

$$
CD^2=AD\cdot DB,
$$

we get

$$
CD^2=(4)(9)=36.
$$

Therefore,

$$
CD=6.
$$

### Example 5: Triangle DIH

Let $ABC$ be an acute triangle with orthocenter $H$. Let $D$ and $I$ be the feet of the altitudes from $A$ and $B$, respectively.

Find $\angle DIH$.

Since $I$ lies on altitude $BI$, we have

$$
BI\perp AC.
$$

Also, $HD$ lies on altitude $AD$, so

$$
HD\perp BC.
$$

Because $DI$ lies along the side connecting two feet of altitudes, angle chasing shows that $D$, $I$, $B$, and $H$ lie on a common circle.

Therefore,

$$
\angle DIH=\angle DBH=90^\circ.
$$

Thus,

$$
\boxed{\angle DIH=90^\circ}.
$$

## Strategy Checklist

* Mark which segment is the base for the altitude you need.
* Use $A=\tfrac{1}{2}bh$ before introducing extra variables.
* Decide whether the triangle is acute, right, or obtuse before placing the orthocenter.
* Look for right triangles created by altitudes.
* Check for similar triangles whenever an altitude is dropped.
* In right triangles, remember the altitude-to-hypotenuse relationships.
* When multiple altitudes appear, search for cyclic quadrilaterals.

## Common Pitfalls

* Forgetting the factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ in the area formula.
* Confusing an altitude with a median.
* Using the wrong base for a given altitude.
* Assuming the orthocenter is always inside the triangle.
* Missing similar triangles created by an altitude.
* Forgetting that an altitude may land on an extension of a side in an obtuse triangle.
* Overlooking cyclic quadrilaterals formed by altitude feet and the orthocenter.
