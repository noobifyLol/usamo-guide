## Overview

Graph theory problems often rely on invariants such as degree sums and
connectivity.

## Key Ideas

- Handshaking lemma: the sum of degrees equals $2E$.
- Trees with $n$ vertices have $n-1$ edges.
- Parity arguments are common in graph proofs.

## Worked Example

Show that any graph has an even number of vertices of odd degree.

The sum of degrees is $2E$, an even number. A sum of degrees is even only if
the count of odd terms is even.

## More Examples (Warm-up):

### Example 1: Interesting Degrees Problem

Let $G$ be a simple graph on $n$ vertices with degrees $d_1,d_2,\dots,d_n$. Suppose that

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i^2 > n\left\lfloor \frac{n^2}{4}\right\rfloor.
$$

Your job is to prove that $G$ contains a triangle.

First, we assume $G$ is triangle-free. Then, by Mantel's Theorem, we have that

$$
m\le \left\lfloor \frac{n^2}{4}\right\rfloor.
$$

Also,

$$
\sum_v d(v)^2=\sum_{uv\in E}(d(u)+d(v)).
$$

For every edge $uv$, we have $d(u)+d(v)\le n$, otherwise $u$ and $v$ would have a common neighbor and form a triangle. Thus,

$$
\sum_v d(v)^2\le nm\le n\left\lfloor \frac{n^2}{4}\right\rfloor,
$$

which contradicts the claim, so therefore $G$ contains a triangle.

### Example 2: Hall's Matching Theorem (classic!)

Let $G=(X,Y)$ be a bipartite graph. Suppose that every vertex of $X$ has degree at least $r$, and every vertex of $Y$ has degree at most $r$.

Your job is to prove that $G$ contains a matching saturating (at least one edge for every vertex) $X$.

First of all, for any subset $S\subseteq X$, we can count edges between $S$ and $N(S)$:

$$
r|S|\le e(S,N(S))\le r|N(S)|.
$$

Thus,
$|N(S)| \ge |S|$


Hall's condition holds, so Hall's Theorem guarantees a matching saturating all vertices of $X$, and we are done!

### Example 3: Tricky Paths Problem

Let $G$ be a graph on $n\ge4$ vertices. Suppose that every pair of nonadjacent vertices $u,v$ satisfies the following:

$$
d(u)+d(v)\ge n+1.
$$

Your job is to prove that any two vertices are connected by at least three internally vertex-disjoint paths (make sure you read this carefully!).

First, assume that there is a vertex cut $S$ with $|S|\le2$. Let $A$ and $B$ be distinct components of $G-S$, and here, we will choose $a\in A$, $b\in B$.

Then

$$
d(a)\le |A|-1+|S|,
\qquad
d(b)\le |B|-1+|S|.
$$

From here, Adding and using $|A|+|B|\le n-|S|$ gives us:

$$
d(a)+d(b)\le n+|S|-2\le n,
$$

which contradicts the claim. Thus $\kappa(G)\ge3$, and Menger's Theorem (important!) means that there are at least three internally vertex-disjoint paths between any two vertices, and here, we are done!

### Example 4: Wonderful Bipartite Matching Problem!

Let $G=(X,Y)$ be a bipartite graph with

$$
|X|=|Y|=n.
$$

Suppose every vertex has degree at least $n/2$.

Your job is to prove that $G$ contains a perfect matching (every vertex has exactly 1 edge).

First, by Hall's Theorem, realize that it suffices to show $|N(S)|\ge |S|$ for every $S\subseteq X$.

Assume $|N(S)|<|S|$. Now, notice that since every vertex of $S$ has degree at least $n/2$,

$$
e(S,N(S))\ge \frac n2|S|.
$$

Because $e(S,N(S))\le |S||N(S)|$, we get that:

$$
|N(S)|\ge \frac n2.
$$

Thus $|S|>\frac n2$. Let $T=Y\setminus N(S)$. Then

$$
|T|=n-|N(S)|>n-|S|.
$$

Every vertex of $T$ has degree at least $n/2$ and no neighbors in $S$, so we have:

$$
|X\setminus S|\ge \frac n2.
$$

Hence $|S|\le n/2$, which is a contradiction! Thus, Hall's condition holds, and $G$ has a perfect matching, and we are done!

### Example 5: Turán's Theorem

Let $G$ be a graph on $n$ vertices with more than

$$
\left(1-\frac1r\right)\frac{n^2}{2}
$$

edges.

Your goal is to prove that $G$ contains a clique (you should know this term!) of size $r+1$.

First, we assume that $G$ contains no $K_{r+1}$. So, by Turán's Theorem, we have:

$$
e(G)\le e(T_r(n))
\le
\left(1-\frac1r\right)\frac{n^2}{2}.
$$

However, this contradicts the claim! So, $G$ must contain a clique of size $r+1$, and we are done here!

### Example 6: A Truly Dazzling Triangles Problem :)

Let $G$ be a graph on $n$ vertices with

$$
m>\frac{n^2}{4}.
$$

Your goal is to prove that there exists an edge contained in at least

$$
\frac{2m}{n}-\frac n2
$$

triangles.

First, for an edge $uv$, let

$$
c(uv)=|N(u)\cap N(v)|.
$$

Since

$$
c(uv)\ge d(u)+d(v)-n,
$$

we can sum over all edges to give us

$$
\sum_{uv\in E} c(uv)
\ge
\sum_v d(v)^2-mn.
$$

Then, from here, we can use Cauchy–Schwarz, to get:

$$
\sum_v d(v)^2
\ge
\frac{(2m)^2}{n}.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{uv\in E} c(uv)
\ge
\frac{4m^2}{n}-mn.
$$

Averaging over all $m$ edges,there must be some edge that satisfies

$$
c(uv)\ge
\frac{4m}{n}-n.
$$

Since

$$
\frac{4m}{n}-n
\ge
\frac{2m}{n}-\frac n2,
$$

that edge lies in at least

$$
\frac{2m}{n}-\frac n2
$$

triangles, and we are done!
