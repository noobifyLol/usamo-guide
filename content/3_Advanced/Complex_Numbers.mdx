## Overview

Complex numbers provide a clean language for rotation and roots of unity.
Contest problems often use $z\overline{z}=|z|^2$ and polar form.

## Key Ideas

- $z=re^{i\theta}$ with $r=|z|$ and argument $\theta$.
- $n$th roots of unity satisfy $z^n=1$ and are evenly spaced on the unit circle.
- Multiplication rotates and scales points in the plane.

## Core Skills

### Switch Between Forms

Use $z=a+bi$ for algebra and $z=re^{i\theta}$ for powers and rotations.

### Use Conjugates and Modulus

Apply $z\overline{z}=|z|^2$ to eliminate imaginary parts or compute lengths.

### Use De Moivre

Compute powers and roots quickly using $r^n e^{in\theta}$.

## Worked Example

Compute $(1+i)^4$.

Since $1+i=\sqrt{2}e^{i\pi/4}$, we have
$(1+i)^4=(\sqrt{2})^4 e^{i\pi}=4(-1)=-4$.

## More Examples

### Example 1: Roots of Unity

Find all solutions to $z^3=1$.

$z=e^{2\pi i k/3}$ for $k=0,1,2$.

### Example 2: Modulus

If $z=3-4i$, compute $|z|$.

$5$.

### Example 3: Rotation

If $z$ is multiplied by $e^{i\pi/3}$, what happens geometrically?

It rotates by $60^\circ$ and keeps the same magnitude.

## Even More Examples

### Example 4: Scary Expression

Let ω = cos(2π/13) + i sin(2π/13).

Your job is to compute the following scary-looking expression:

(1 + ω + ω²)(1 + ω² + ω⁴)(1 + ω³ + ω⁶)(1 + ω⁴ + ω⁸)(1 + ω⁵ + ω¹⁰)(1 + ω⁶ + ω¹²)

First of all, notice that we have 

1 + x + x² = (x³ − 1)/(x − 1),

, and so the product becomes

[(ω³ − 1)(ω⁶ − 1)(ω⁹ − 1)(ω¹² − 1)(ω¹⁵ − 1)(ω¹⁸ − 1)]
/
[(ω − 1)(ω² − 1)(ω³ − 1)(ω⁴ − 1)(ω⁵ − 1)(ω⁶ − 1)].

From here, the key observation is that multiplication by 3 essentially PERMUTES the nonzero residues mod 13 (verify this yourself), so the numerator and denominator just contain the same factors!!!

Thus, our answer is simply 1. 

### Example 5: De Moivre + Intimidating Constraint

Assume that complex number(s) z satisfies the following:

z⁵ = 32

and that

Re(z²) < 0.

Your job is to find the sum of ALL possible values of z.

To begin, the roots are

zₖ = 2(cos(360°k/5) + i sin(360°k/5)),
for k = 0,1,2,3,4.

Then, realize that the condition Re(z²) < 0 holds for k = 1,2.

So, we get:

z = 2(cos72° + i sin72°)

and

z = 2(cos144° + i sin144°).

And their sum simplifies to

−1 + i√5!

### Example 6: What is the real part?

Your job is to compute: Re((1 + i)²⁰).

First we write the following:

1 + i = √2(cos45° + i sin45°).

Then, we do a bit of straightforward calulcations to get the answer:

(1 + i)²⁰
= (√2)²⁰(cos900° + i sin900°)
= 2¹⁰(cos180° + i sin180°)
= −1024.

So, our answer is: −1024.

### Example 7: A Complex Complex Equation (you get it lol?)

Your job is to find ALL complex numbers satisfying the following expression:

z⁴ + conj(z)⁴ = 0.

First, we will let

z = r e^(iθ).

Then, notice that

z⁴ + conj(z)⁴
= r⁴ e^(i4θ) + r⁴ e^(-i4θ)
= 2r⁴ cos(4θ).

And so, we have

cos(4θ) = 0.

Which implies that

θ = π/8 + kπ/4.

Therefore, we can now calculate z!

z = re^(i(π/8 + kπ/4)), which is our answer. 

### Example 8: Another Scary Product 

Let

z = cos18° + i sin18°.

Your job is to compute the following expression:

(z − z⁻¹)(z² − z⁻²)(z³ − z⁻³)(z⁴ − z⁻⁴).

First, using the fact that

zⁿ − z⁻ⁿ = 2i sin(nθ),

the expression simpy becomes

(2i)⁴ sin18° sin36° sin54° sin72°.

Now, using the fact below (make sure to prove it yourself):

sin18° sin36° sin54° sin72° = 5/16,

we get that the value of the expression is simply

5.

### Example 9: A No-Name Problem 

Suppose that

z + 1/z = 1 + i.

Your job is to find:

z⁴ + 1/z⁴.

First, we will let

aₙ = zⁿ + 1/zⁿ.

From which we get the following relation:

aₙ₊₁ = a₁aₙ − aₙ₋₁,

where we have that

a₁ = 1 + i.

Now, we just need to plug and chug and compute!

a₂ = (1 + i)² − 2 = 2i − 2,

a₃ = (1 + i)(2i − 2) − (1 + i) = −3 − 3i,

a₄ = (1 + i)(−3 − 3i) − (2i − 2).

Thus, the answer is -4 - 8i!
