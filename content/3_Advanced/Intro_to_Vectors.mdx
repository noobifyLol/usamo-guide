## Overview

A **vector** is any quantity that has both magnitude and direction. For example, a description of the wind in a weather report typically includes both the strength of the wind and the direction from which the wind is blowing, making wind a vector quantity.

Many important quantities in science can be described as vectors, especially in physics. For example, a force acting on an object is a vector, since it has both magnitude (the strength of the force) and direction (the direction of the push or pull). Other vector quantities in physics include momentum, impulse, angular momentum, torque, and the electric and magnetic field vectors.

In mathematics, vectors are indispensable tools in geometry, and are used extensively to provide geometric insight in subjects such as multivariable calculus and differential equations. Vectors and their generalizations are also one of the main objects of study in linear algebra.

## Key Ideas

- Two vectors are considered equal as long as they have the same magnitude and the same direction, even if they start and end at different points.
- Ordinary numerical quantities (real numbers) are referred to as **scalars**; a variable may be either a **vector variable** or a **scalar variable**.
- Vector addition obeys the commutative, associative, and additive identity properties.
- The **negation** $-\mathbf{v}$ has the same magnitude as $\mathbf{v}$ but the exact opposite direction; $\mathbf{v} + (-\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$.
- A **linear combination** of vectors $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ is any expression of the form $a\mathbf{v} + b\mathbf{w}$ where $a$ and $b$ are scalars.
- In $\mathbb{R}^2$, every vector can be written as a linear combination of the standard basis vectors $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$.
- A **unit vector** has magnitude equal to 1; given any nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$, its normalization is $\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{v}|}\mathbf{v}$.

### Notation and Terminology

When discussing vectors, ordinary numerical quantities (i.e. real numbers) are referred to as **scalars**. Both vectors and scalars are used for algebra, and a variable may be either a **vector variable** or a **scalar variable**.

To help distinguish between these two types of quantities, vector variables will always be written in an upright bold font:

$$\mathbf{u}, \quad \mathbf{v}, \quad \text{or} \quad \mathbf{w},$$

while scalar variables will be written in the usual italic font:

$$x, \quad y, \quad \text{or} \quad z.$$

This is the most popular convention for distinguishing between vector and scalar variables, but other conventions are possible. For example, some books denote a vector variable using an arrow above the letter:

$$\vec{u}, \quad \vec{v}, \quad \text{or} \quad \vec{w}.$$

This is also the most common way to indicate a vector variable when doing hand calculations, since it is difficult to make handwritten letters bold, and the reader is encouraged to adopt this practice.

### Magnitude of a Vector

The **magnitude** of a vector is a scalar, and corresponds to the length of any arrow that represents the vector. Different books write the magnitude of a vector $\mathbf{v}$ in different ways:

$$|\mathbf{v}|, \quad \|\mathbf{v}\|, \quad \text{or} \quad v.$$

Also, some books refer to the magnitude of a vector as its **length** or **norm** instead. We will consistently use the word "magnitude" and write $|\mathbf{v}|$ for the magnitude of a vector $\mathbf{v}$.

The magnitude of a vector $\mathbf{v}$ is always either positive or zero:

$$|\mathbf{v}| \geq 0$$

If $|\mathbf{v}| = 0$, then $\mathbf{v}$ is the **zero vector**, which has a magnitude of zero but no direction. We will use a bold zero $\mathbf{0}$ to denote the zero vector.

**Properties of Vector Addition**

Vector addition has the following properties:

1. Commutative property: $\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{w} + \mathbf{v}$ for any two vectors $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$.
2. Associative property: $(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) + \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{u} + (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w})$ for any three vectors $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$.
3. Additive identity: $\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{v}$ for any vector $\mathbf{v}$, where $\mathbf{0}$ is the zero vector.

### Negation and Subtraction

The **negation** of a vector $\mathbf{v}$, denoted $-\mathbf{v}$, is the vector with the same magnitude but the exact opposite direction. Sometimes $-\mathbf{v}$ is also called the **opposite** of $\mathbf{v}$, and together $\mathbf{v}$ and $-\mathbf{v}$ are **opposite vectors**. Note that the negation of the zero vector is again the zero vector.

A vector and its negation are additive inverses, meaning that

$$\mathbf{v} + (-\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$$

for any vector $\mathbf{v}$.

Although this operation is referred to as negation, there isn't any sense in which a vector is either positive or negative. A vector $\mathbf{v}$ points in a certain direction, and its negation $-\mathbf{v}$ points in the exact opposite direction, but neither of these directions is preferred, and there is no good way to decide which of $\mathbf{v}$ and $-\mathbf{v}$ should be positive and which should be negative.

We can use negation to define subtraction for vectors. If $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ are vectors, the **difference** $\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{v}$ is defined by the formula

$$\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{w} + (-\mathbf{v}).$$

If $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ are arranged to have the same beginning point, then the difference $\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{v}$ is the vector that points from the end of $\mathbf{v}$ to the end of $\mathbf{w}$.

### Scalar Multiplication

There does not seem to be a good way of multiplying two vectors in general. However, there is an operation called **scalar multiplication** that allows us to multiply a vector by a scalar.

For a positive scalar $k$ and a vector $\mathbf{v}$, the product $k\mathbf{v}$ is a vector in the same direction as $\mathbf{v}$ whose magnitude has been scaled by a factor of $k$. Thus,

$$|k\mathbf{v}| = k|\mathbf{v}|.$$

Multiplying a vector by a negative scalar both scales and negates the vector. Thus $(-1)\mathbf{v}$ is the same as $-\mathbf{v}$, and $(-k)\mathbf{v}$ is the same as $-(k\mathbf{v})$ for any scalar $k$. Note then that

$$|k\mathbf{v}| = |k||\mathbf{v}|$$

for any scalar $k$, where $|k|$ is the absolute value of $k$.

Finally, multiplying a vector by the scalar $0$ results in the zero vector:

$$0\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$

**Properties of Scalar Multiplication**

Scalar multiplication has the following properties:

1. Associativity: $a(b\mathbf{v}) = (ab)\mathbf{v}$ for any scalars $a$, $b$ and any vector $\mathbf{v}$.
2. Multiplicative identity: $1\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$ for any vector $\mathbf{v}$.
3. Left distributivity: $a(\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}) = a\mathbf{v} + a\mathbf{w}$ for any scalar $a$ and any vectors $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$.
4. Right distributivity: $(a + b)\mathbf{v} = a\mathbf{v} + b\mathbf{v}$ for any scalars $a$, $b$ and any vector $\mathbf{v}$.

$$a\mathbf{v} + b\mathbf{w},$$

where $a$ and $b$ are scalars.

A linear combination is the most general way of combining two vectors $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ using vector addition and multiplication by scalars. That is, any expression involving $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ and these two operations can always be simplified algebraically to a linear combination of $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$.

It is also possible to take a linear combination of more than two vectors. For example, if $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, and $\mathbf{w}$ are three vectors, a linear combination of them would have the form

$$a\mathbf{u} + b\mathbf{v} + c\mathbf{w},$$

where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are scalars. More generally, if $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$ are $n$ different vectors, then a linear combination of them has the form

$$a_1\mathbf{v}_1 + a_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + a_n\mathbf{v}_n,$$

where $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n$ are $n$ scalars.

### The Algebra of Vectors

The properties of vector addition and scalar multiplication mean that essentially all of the usual rules of algebra apply to vectors, as long we do not try to multiply two vectors together or add a vector to a scalar. In particular, we can use algebra to solve equations involving vectors.

Express the vector $2(-3\mathbf{v} + 4\mathbf{w}) - 3(-5\mathbf{v})$ as a linear combination of $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$.

**Solution** Distributing the $2$ and $-3$ through gives

$$-6\mathbf{v} + 8\mathbf{w} + 15\mathbf{v},$$

and combining like terms yields the linear combination

$$9\mathbf{v} + 8\mathbf{w}.$$

### Column Vectors

There is another common notation for linear combinations of $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$. Given any scalars $a$ and $b$, we let

$$\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = a\,\mathbf{i} + b\,\mathbf{j}.$$

That is, $\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}$ is a vector whose components are $a$ and $b$. A vector written in this fashion is known as a **column vector**. This way of writing vectors is very popular within mathematics, but physicists usually prefer to write $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$ explicitly.

Of course, any vector $\mathbf{v}$ can be written as a column vector, with

$$\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}$$

Note that the standard basis vectors $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$ can themselves be written as column vectors. In particular, since $\mathbf{i} = 1\mathbf{i} + 0\mathbf{j}$ and $\mathbf{j} = 0\mathbf{i} + 1\mathbf{j}$, we have

$$\mathbf{i} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \mathbf{j} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

### Vector Operations

The operations of vector addition and scalar multiplication are easy to compute using components. The formula for vector addition is

$$\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a + c \\ b + d \end{bmatrix}$$

That is, each component of the sum of two vectors is the sum of the corresponding components of the two vectors. For example,

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix}.$$

The formula for scalar multiplication is equally simple:

$$k\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ka \\ kb \end{bmatrix}$$

That is, multiplying a vector by a scalar $k$ is the same as multiplying each component by $k$. For example,

$$2\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 8 \end{bmatrix}.$$

### Vector Equations

Since the components of a vector determine both the magnitude and direction, two vectors are equal if and only if they have the same components.

**Equality of Vectors**

The equation

$$\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \end{bmatrix}$$

holds if and only if $a = c$ and $b = d$.

Here **if and only if** means that either statement implies the other. That is, if two vectors are equal then their components are the same, and if two vectors have the same components then they must be equal.

A **vector equation** is an equation for which both sides are vectors. The principle above lets us separate a vector equation into two scalar equations, which is often necessary for finding the solution.

### Vectors in Science

In science, vectors are used to represent any quantity that has both magnitude and direction. For example:

1. Any moving object has an associated **velocity vector**. The magnitude of this vector is the speed of motion, and the direction is the direction in which the object is moving.

2. In physics, a force acting on an object is represented by a **force vector**. The magnitude of this vector is the strength of the force, and the direction is the direction in which the force pushes.

Note that the magnitude of a vector may have any units, in which case the components of a vector have those same units. For example, the magnitude and components of a velocity vector have units of m/sec, while the magnitude and components of a force vector are measured in Newtons (N).

Vectors in science are often drawn as arrows, even if the vectors themselves do not have units of length. For example, a free body diagram in physics represents the forces acting on an object as arrows. In this case, the scale of the arrows is arbitrary, as long as all force vectors are drawn to the same scale.

## Examples

### Example 1

Find the magnitude and direction of the vector $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution** From the formulas above, we have

$$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{(-4)^2 + (3)^2} = 5 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \tan\theta = -\frac{3}{4},$$

where $\theta$ is the direction of $\mathbf{v}$. Now,

$$\tan^{-1}\!\left(-\frac{3}{4}\right) = -36.87°,$$

but this is not the right direction, since the vector would have to point down and to the right. Adding $180°$ gives the correct direction of $143.13°$.

### Example 2

Solve for $x$:

$$3\begin{bmatrix} x \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ -6 \end{bmatrix} = x\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}.$$

**Solution** Multiplying the left vector through by $3$ and the right vector through by $x$ gives the equation

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3x \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ -6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2x \\ 3x \end{bmatrix}.$$

We can now subtract the two vectors on the left to get

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3x - 4 \\ 12 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2x \\ 3x \end{bmatrix},$$

which is the same as the two scalar equations

$$3x - 4 = 2x \qquad \text{and} \qquad 12 = 3x.$$

The solution to both of these equations is $x = 4$.

### Example 3

Solve for $x$ and $y$:

$$x\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + y\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}.$$

**Solution** Combining the vectors on the left together gives the equation

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2x + 2y \\ 3x + y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix},$$

which is the same as the system of equations

$$2x + 2y = 8 \qquad \text{and} \qquad 3x + y = 6.$$

The second equation tells us that $y = 6 - 3x$. Plugging this into the first equation and solving for $x$ yields $x = 1$, and it follows that $y = 3$.

When writing unit vectors by hand, it is common to replace the arrow above the letter with a hat, i.e. $\hat{u}$ instead of $\vec{u}$. Given any nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$, we can find a unit vector $\mathbf{u}$ in the same direction as $\mathbf{v}$ using the formula

$$\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{v}|}\mathbf{v}$$

Although we think of a point and its radial vector very differently, they are both described by the same pair of real numbers $x$, $y$. This makes it possible to regard points and vectors as the same thing. Indeed, from now on we will make **no distinction between a point in the plane and its radial vector**. Thus

$$( x, y ) = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}$$

for any real numbers $x$ and $y$.

This convention simplifies our mathematics, because it decreases the number of different kinds of objects that we have to contend with. It also gives us the freedom to use vector operations such as addition and scalar multiplication on points as well as vectors. For example, we can now add two vectors, add two points, or add a vector to a point without worrying about the distinction between these operations.

### Turning Vectors 90°

Many geometry problems in the plane involve two perpendicular directions, so it can be helpful to know the rule for turning a vector $90°$. As you can see, **you can turn a vector $90°$ by switching the two components and negating one of them**.

Of course, the direction in which you turn the vector depends on which of the two components you negate. Specifically, switching the two components and then negating the first component turns a vector $90°$ counterclockwise, while switching the two components and then negating the second component turns a vector $90°$ clockwise.

## Examples

### Example 1

Find a unit vector that is parallel to the line $y = \frac{1}{2}x + 1$.

**Solution** This line has slope $1/2$, so it is parallel to the vector $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$. Since

$$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{2^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{5},$$

the desired unit vector is

$$\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{v}|}\mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.$$

### Example 2

Find a vector in the same direction as $\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$ that has magnitude 3.

**Solution** Let $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$. Then $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5$, so

$$\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{v}|}\mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

is a unit vector in the same direction as $\mathbf{v}$. Multiplying $\mathbf{u}$ by 3 gives the desired vector:

$$3\mathbf{u} = \frac{3}{5}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9/5 \\ 12/5 \end{bmatrix}.$$

**Solution** We wish to find the vector $\mathbf{v}$ that stretches from the point $(2, 3)$ to the center of the circle. We know that the magnitude of $\mathbf{v}$ is 2, so we need only find its direction.

The direction of $\mathbf{v}$ is perpendicular to that of the line $y = 9 - 3x$. This line has slope $-3$, and is therefore parallel to the vector $(-1, 3)$. Turning this vector $90°$ clockwise, we conclude that $\mathbf{v}$ is parallel to the vector $(3, 1)$.

All that remains is to scale the vector $(3, 1)$ to have length 2. Since

$$|(3, 1)| = \sqrt{3^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{10},$$

the vector $\frac{1}{\sqrt{10}}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ is a unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$. Since $|\mathbf{v}| = 2$, we conclude that

$$\mathbf{v} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{10}}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.$$

Then

$$\mathbf{p} = (2, 3) + \mathbf{v} = (2, 3) + \frac{2}{\sqrt{10}}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \left(2 + \frac{6}{\sqrt{10}},\ 3 + \frac{2}{\sqrt{10}}\right).$$
