## Overview

Distribution problems ask us to place objects into boxes, give objects to people, or assign items to recipients.
The main questions are:
- Are the objects distinct or identical?
- Are the recipients distinct?
- Can someone receive nothing?
- Are there lower or upper bounds?

The answers determine which counting method we use.

## Motivation

Suppose we want to distribute $5$ identical sweets among $3$ children.

If the children are named Alice, Ben, and Chloe, then an outcome is determined
by three nonnegative integers:

$$
x_1+x_2+x_3=5.
$$

Here, $x_1$ is the number of sweets Alice receives, $x_2$ is the number Ben
receives, and $x_3$ is the number Chloe receives.

This is different from splitting sweets into unlabelled groups. The children
are distinct, so

$$
(5,0,0)
$$

and

$$
(0,5,0)
$$

are different outcomes.

This is the setting where stars and bars becomes useful.

## Distinct Objects to Distinct People

Suppose there are $n$ distinct objects and $r$ distinct people.

If each object can go to any person, then each object has $r$ choices.

So the number of distributions is

$$
r^n.
$$

### Example 1

> How many ways are there to distribute $6$ distinct books among $3$ students?

Each book can go to any of the $3$ students.

Therefore, the number of ways is

$$
3^6.
$$

## Distinct Objects With No Empty Recipient

If every person must receive at least one distinct object, the problem is more
subtle.

For small cases, we often use casework or inclusion exclusion.

### Example 2

> How many ways are there to distribute $4$ distinct books among $2$ students
> so that each student receives at least one book?

Without restriction, each book has $2$ choices, so there are

$$
2^4=16
$$

ways.

Now subtract the cases where one student gets no books.

There are $2$ such bad cases:
- All books go to the first student
- All books go to the second student

So the answer is

$$
16-2=14.
$$

## Identical Objects to Distinct People

Suppose we want to distribute $n$ identical objects among $r$ distinct people,
and people are allowed to receive nothing.

This is the number of nonnegative integer solutions to

$$
x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_r=n.
$$

By stars and bars, the number of solutions is

$$
\binom{n+r-1}{r-1}.
$$

### Why Stars and Bars Works

Imagine $n$ stars representing the objects.

We need to divide them into $r$ groups, one for each person. This requires
$r-1$ dividers.

So we arrange

$$
n
$$

stars and

$$
r-1
$$

bars in a row.

The total number of symbols is

$$
n+r-1.
$$

Choosing the positions of the bars determines the distribution, so the number
of distributions is

$$
\binom{n+r-1}{r-1}.
$$

## At Least One Each

If each of the $r$ people must receive at least one identical object, then we
need positive integer solutions to

$$
x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_r=n.
$$

Give each person one object first. This uses $r$ objects.

Now distribute the remaining

$$
n-r
$$

objects freely among the $r$ people.

The number of ways is

$$
\binom{n-1}{r-1}.
$$

### Example 3

> How many ways are there to distribute $10$ identical sweets among $4$
> children if each child must receive at least one sweet?

We need positive integer solutions to

$$
x_1+x_2+x_3+x_4=10.
$$

By stars and bars, the answer is

$$
\binom{10-1}{4-1}
=
\binom{9}{3}
=
84.
$$

## Upper Bounds

Sometimes each person can receive at most a certain number of objects.

These problems often require casework or complementary counting.

### Example 4

> How many ways are there to distribute $8$ identical sweets among $3$ children if no child receives more than $4$ sweets?

First ignore the upper bound.

The number of nonnegative solutions to

$$
x_1+x_2+x_3=8
$$

is

$$
\binom{10}{2}=45.
$$

Now subtract distributions where some child receives at least $5$ sweets.

Suppose

$$
x_1\ge 5.
$$

Let

$$
y_1=x_1-5.
$$

Then

$$
y_1+x_2+x_3=3.
$$

The number of solutions is

$$
\binom{5}{2}=10.
$$

The same applies to each of the $3$ children, so we subtract

$$
3(10)=30.
$$

No two children can both receive at least $5$ sweets, since that would require
at least $10$ sweets.

So the answer is

$$
45-30=15.
$$

## Mixtures of Object Types

Some problems involve multiple types of identical objects.

For example, suppose we distribute $5$ identical red balls and $3$ identical blue balls among $2$ children.

We can distribute the red balls independently of the blue balls.

The red balls can be distributed in

$$
\binom{5+2-1}{2-1}=6
$$

ways.

The blue balls can be distributed in

$$
\binom{3+2-1}{2-1}=4
$$

ways.

Therefore, the total number of distributions is

$$
6(4)=24.
$$

## Worked Examples

### Example 5

> How many ways are there to distribute $7$ identical balls among $3$ labelled
> boxes?

We need nonnegative integer solutions to

$$
x_1+x_2+x_3=7.
$$

By stars and bars, the answer is

$$
\binom{7+3-1}{3-1}
=
\binom{9}{2}
=
36.
$$

### Example 6

> How many ways are there to distribute $7$ identical balls among $3$ labelled
> boxes if each box must contain at least one ball?

We need positive integer solutions to

$$
x_1+x_2+x_3=7.
$$

The answer is

$$
\binom{7-1}{3-1}
=
\binom{6}{2}
=
15.
$$

### Example 7

> How many ways are there to distribute $5$ distinct books among $3$ students if
> one particular student must receive exactly $2$ books?

Choose the $2$ books for that student:

$$
\binom{5}{2}.
$$

Each of the remaining $3$ books can go to either of the other two students, so
there are

$$
2^3
$$

ways.

So the answer is

$$
\binom{5}{2}2^3=10(8)=80.
$$

## Key Contest Insight

Distribution problems depend heavily on whether the objects and recipients are
distinct.

If the objects are distinct and the recipients aredistinct, think about choices
for each object.

If the objects are identical and the recipients are distinct, think about
integer solutions and stars and bars.

If there are restrictions, first count without the restriction, then subtract
the invalid cases when possible.

## Strategy Tips
- Identify whether the objects are distinct or identical
- Identify whether the recipients are distinct
- For distinct objects to distinct people, use choices for each object
- For identical objects to distinct people, use stars and bars
- For 'at least one each', give one object to each person first
- For upper bounds, use complementary counting or casework
- For multiple independent object types, count each type separately and multiply

## Common Pitfalls

- Confusing distribution to labelled people with splitting into unlabelled
  groups
- Using stars and bars for distinct objects
- Forgetting that people may be allowed to receive nothing
- Forgetting to handle the 'at least one' condition
- Ignoring upper bounds
- Multiplying independent counts when the choices are not actually independent
