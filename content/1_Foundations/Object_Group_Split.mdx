## Overview

Many counting problems ask us to split objects into groups.

For example:

- Divide $10$ students into two teams of $5$
- Split $12$ people into groups of sizes $3,4,5$
- Separate objects into groups where the groups may or may not be labelled
- Divide objects with repeated types into smaller collections

The main challenge is deciding whether the groups are labelled.

If the groups have names, such as Team A and Team B, then the groups are
labelled.

If the groups have no names, then the groups are unlabelled, and we have to avoid
counting the same grouping multiple times.

## Motivation

Suppose $8$ students are split into two teams of $4$.

If the teams are called Red Team and Blue Team, then choosing the Red Team
automatically determines the Blue Team.

The number of ways is

$$
\binom{8}{4}.
$$

However, if the two teams are not named, then choosing one group of $4$ first
and the other group second counts every split twice.

So the number of unlabelled splits is

$$
\frac{1}{2}\binom{8}{4}.
$$

This difference between labelled and unlabelled groups is one of the most common
sources of mistakes in combinatorics.

## Labelled Groups

Suppose we have $n$ distinct objects and want to split them into labelled groups
of sizes

$$
n_1,n_2,\ldots,n_k,
$$

where

$$
n_1+n_2+\cdots+n_k=n.
$$

The number of ways is

$$
\frac{n!}{n_1!n_2!\cdots n_k!}.
$$

This is because we choose which objects go into the first group, then the second
group, and so on.

### Example 1

> How many ways are there to split $10$ students into three labelled groups of
> sizes $2,3,5$?

The groups are labelled, so the answer is

$$
\frac{10!}{2!3!5!}.
$$

Equivalently, we can choose:

$$
\binom{10}{2}
$$

students for the first group, then

$$
\binom{8}{3}
$$

students for the second group, and the remaining $5$ students form the third
group.

As such, the answer is

$$
\binom{10}{2}\binom{8}{3}
=
\frac{10!}{2!3!5!}.
$$

## Unlabelled Groups of Different Sizes

If the group sizes are all different, then the sizes themselves identify the
groups.

For example, if we split $9$ people into groups of sizes $2,3,4$, then the
group of size $2$ cannot be confused with the group of size $3$.

So the number of ways is still

$$
\frac{9!}{2!3!4!}.
$$

No extra division is needed.

### Example 2

> How many ways are there to split $9$ students into unlabelled groups of sizes
> $2,3,4$?

Since the group sizes are different, the answer is

$$
\frac{9!}{2!3!4!}.
$$

## Unlabelled Groups of Equal Sizes

If some groups have the same size and are unlabelled, then we must divide by
the number of ways to reorder those equal sized groups.

### Example 3

> How many ways are there to split $8$ students into two unlabelled groups of
> $4$?

First choose $4$ students for one group:

$$
\binom{8}{4}.
$$

But this counts each split twice, because choosing the first group or choosing
its complement gives the same final split.

So the answer is

$$
\frac{1}{2}\binom{8}{4}=35.
$$

### Example 4

> How many ways are there to split $12$ students into three unlabelled groups of
> $4$?

If the groups were labelled, the answer would be

$$
\frac{12!}{4!4!4!}.
$$

But the three groups are unlabelled, so we divide by

$$
3!.
$$

So the answer is

$$
\frac{12!}{4!4!4!3!}.
$$

## Mixed Group Sizes

Sometimes only some groups have equal sizes.

### Example 5

> How many ways are there to split $11$ students into unlabelled groups of
> sizes $2,2,3,4$?

If the groups were labelled, the answer would be

$$
\frac{11!}{2!2!3!4!}.
$$

The two groups of size $2$ are indistinguishable, so we divide by

$$
2!.
$$

The groups of size $3$ and $4$ are unique by size, so no further division is
needed.

So the answer is

$$
\frac{11!}{2!2!3!4!2!}.
$$

## Identical Objects and Groups

If the objects are identical and the groups are unlabelled, the problem becomes
a partition problem.

For example, splitting $7$ identical balls into $3$ unlabelled nonempty groups
means finding unordered positive integer triples whose sum is $7$.

The possible group sizes are

$$
1,1,5
$$

$$
1,2,4
$$

$$
1,3,3
$$

$$
2,2,3.
$$

So there are $4$ ways.

Unlike labelled distribution, there is no simple single formula for every
unlabelled identical object grouping problem. These are often handled using
casework.

## Mixtures of Object Types

Some problems involve repeated types of objects.

For example, suppose we have:
- $3$ red balls
- $2$ blue balls
- $1$ green ball

If we want to split them into groups, we must track how many objects of each
type go into each group.

These problems are usually solved by:

1. Deciding the possible type counts in each group
2. Counting valid cases
3. Dividing by symmetries if the groups are unlabelled

The key is to avoid treating identical objects as distinct.

## Worked Examples

### Example 6

> How many ways are there to split $6$ students into three unlabelled pairs?

If the pairs were labelled, the answer would be

$$
\frac{6!}{2!2!2!}.
$$

But the three pairs are unlabelled, so divide by

$$
3!.
$$

The answer is

$$
\frac{6!}{2!2!2!3!}=15.
$$

### Example 7

> How many ways are there to split $10$ students into two unlabelled groups of
> $3$ and one group of $4$?

If the groups were labelled, the answer would be

$$
\frac{10!}{3!3!4!}.
$$

The two groups of size $3$ are indistinguishable, so divide by

$$
2!.
$$

So the answer is

$$
\frac{10!}{3!3!4!2!}.
$$

### Example 8

> How many ways are there to split $5$ identical balls into two unlabelled non empty groups?

We need unordered positive pairs adding to $5$.

The possibilities are

$$
1+4
$$

and

$$
2+3.
$$

So there are $2$ ways.

## Key Contest Insight

When splitting into groups, first decide whether the groups are labelled.

If the groups are labelled, choose objects for each group directly.

If the groups are unlabelled, count as though they were labelled and then divide
by the number of ways to permute identical groups.

The most common mistake is forgetting to divide when groups of the same size are
unlabelled.

## Strategy Tips
- Decide whether the objects are distinct or identical
- Decide whether the groups are labelled or unlabelled
- For distinct objects and labelled groups, use factorial division
- For unlabelled equal sized groups, divide by the factorial of the number of
  identical groups
- If group sizes are all different, no extra division is needed
- For identical objects into unlabelled groups, use casework or partitions
- Always check whether two counted outcomes are actually the same grouping

## Common Pitfalls
- Treating unlabelled groups as labelled
- Dividing by too much when group sizes are different
- Forgetting to divide by $3!$ when forming three identical groups
- Treating identical objects as distinct
- Confusing grouping with distribution to named people
- Missing cases when identical objects are split into unlabelled groups
